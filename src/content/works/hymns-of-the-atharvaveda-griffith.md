# The Hymns of the Atharvaveda

## Overview

The Atharvaveda, translated into English by Ralph T. H. Griffith between 1895 and 1896, represents the fourth and most distinctive of the four canonical Vedas. While the Rigveda, Samaveda, and Yajurveda primarily focus on liturgy, sacrifice, and hymns to the gods, the Atharvaveda stands apart for its practical and esoteric content addressing the concerns of daily life in Vedic society.

Composed during the later Vedic period (approximately 1200-1000 BCE), the Atharvaveda contains 730 hymns organized into 20 books (kandas) with approximately 6,000 stanzas. The text encompasses spells for healing physical ailments, charms for protection against demons and enemies, incantations for securing love and prosperity, curses to harm adversaries, philosophical hymns addressing metaphysical questions, and botanical knowledge about medicinal plants.

The Atharvaveda has been characterized as representing "popular religion" in contrast to the "hieratic religion" of the other three Vedas. Its content reflects the magical and medicinal practices of ordinary people rather than exclusively focusing on the elaborate sacrificial ceremonies conducted by elite priestly classes. This distinction made the Atharvaveda controversial in ancient India, with some orthodox traditions initially refusing to recognize it as canonical Veda alongside the trayi vidya (threefold knowledge) of Rig, Sama, and Yajur Vedas.

Griffith's translation, published by E. J. Lazarus in Benares between 1895-1896, made this distinctive Vedic text accessible to English-speaking audiences for the first time in complete form. His work complemented his earlier Rigveda translation and his subsequent translations of the Samaveda and Yajurveda, making him the first Western scholar to render all four Vedas into English.

## About Ralph T. H. Griffith

Ralph Thomas Hotchkin Griffith (1826-1906) dedicated his scholarly career to translating ancient Sanskrit literature into English, with particular focus on the Vedic corpus. Born on May 25, 1826, in England, Griffith served as a member of the Indian Education Service, spending significant portions of his career in India where he gained direct exposure to Sanskrit learning traditions and the living practice of Vedic recitation.

His translation of the Atharvaveda came relatively late in his career, published when he was in his late sixties, following his complete Rigveda translation (1889-1896). The Atharvaveda presented distinctive challenges compared to other Vedic texts, with its archaic language, technical magical terminology, and references to folk practices requiring specialized knowledge for accurate interpretation.

Griffith completed translations of all four Vedas: the Rigveda (1889-1896), Samaveda (1893), Yajurveda (1899), and Atharvaveda (1895-1896). This comprehensive engagement with Vedic literature was unprecedented among Western scholars and demonstrated remarkable linguistic facility and scholarly dedication spanning decades.

Like his other Vedic translations, Griffith's Atharvaveda work relied on traditional Sanskrit commentaries, particularly that of Sayana, while attempting to render the text in readable English that conveyed something of the original's character. He died on November 7, 1906, leaving a substantial body of translation work that continued to serve students and scholars throughout the 20th century.

## Structure and Content

The Atharvaveda exists in two main recensions: the Shaunakiya (Paippalada) and the Paippalada, with the Shaunakiya being the more widely known and studied. Griffith's translation follows the Shaunakiya recension, which comprises 730 hymns organized into 20 books containing approximately 6,000 stanzas.

### Organization by Books

The text's 20 books vary considerably in length and content. Books 1-7 contain the oldest material, with hymns addressing practical concerns through spells and charms. Books 8-12 include longer, more philosophical hymns, some borrowed from the Rigveda. Books 13-18 focus on specific themes including marriage, funeral rites, and royal consecration. Books 19-20 appear to be later additions, with Book 20 consisting entirely of hymns borrowed from the Rigveda.

### Thematic Categories

**Healing and Medicine**: Numerous hymns address physical ailments including fevers, headaches, wounds, skin diseases, and internal disorders. These healing spells invoke medicinal plants, divine physicians (particularly the Ashvins), and cosmic powers to cure suffering. The Atharvaveda represents one of the oldest surviving records of Indo-European folk healing traditions and reveals the "earliest forms of folk healing of Indo-European antiquity."

**Protection and Welfare**: Many charms seek protection against demons (rakshasas), sorcerers, enemies, wild animals, snakes, and other threats. Protective amulets are consecrated through these hymns, which invoke divine guardians and cosmic powers to shield the supplicant from harm. These protection spells reflect the anxieties and dangers of Vedic society, where supernatural threats were perceived as real and immediate.

**Love and Prosperity**: The text includes incantations to secure romantic love, maintain marital harmony, ensure fertility, attract prosperity, and achieve success in various undertakings. These spells often invoke Kama (desire), Agni (fire), and other deities associated with generative powers and material well-being.

**Curses and Malevolent Magic**: Unlike the predominantly benevolent orientation of other Vedas, the Atharvaveda contains spells explicitly designed to harm enemies, cause disease, destroy rivals, and exact revenge. This malevolent dimension reflects the text's grounding in practical social realities where magical warfare complemented physical conflict.

**Philosophical Hymns**: Interspersed among practical spells are sophisticated philosophical compositions addressing cosmogony, the nature of brahman (ultimate reality), the structure of time, and metaphysical speculation. These philosophical hymns link the Atharvaveda to the Upanishadic tradition and demonstrate that practical magic and abstract philosophy coexisted in Vedic thought.

### Distinctive Features

The Atharvaveda's language exhibits greater archaism than the Rigveda in certain respects, preserving older forms and vocabulary. However, it also contains later linguistic developments, suggesting compilation over an extended period. The text employs specialized vocabulary for magical procedures, medicinal plants, and anatomical terms not found in other Vedic texts.

Many hymns include detailed instructions for ritual performance, specifying materials, gestures, and timing essential for magical efficacy. This practical orientation distinguishes the Atharvaveda from the more abstractly liturgical character of the Rigveda and Samaveda.

The text freely borrows from the Rigveda, with approximately one-sixth of its verses derived from that earlier collection. However, borrowed verses often appear in new contexts, their meaning transformed from liturgical praise to magical application.

## Ritual Context and Practice

The Atharvaveda served distinct ritual purposes compared to the three liturgical Vedas. While the Rigveda, Samaveda, and Yajurveda centered on elaborate public sacrifices (shrauta rituals) performed by specialized priests, the Atharvaveda primarily addressed domestic rituals (grhya rites) and individual concerns.

### Priestly Specialization

The fourth priest in major Vedic sacrifices, the Brahma, drew from the Atharvaveda to correct ritual errors and ensure ceremonial purity. This supervisory role reflected the Atharvaveda's association with protective and corrective knowledge rather than direct sacrificial action.

However, many Atharvaveda hymns functioned outside the context of elaborate public sacrifice. Householders, local healers, and folk practitioners employed these spells in domestic settings, agricultural contexts, and community healing practices. This broader social distribution distinguished Atharvan knowledge from the more restricted domain of sacrificial specialists.

### Magical Procedures

Atharvaveda rituals often involved material objects serving as magical instruments: amulets, herbs, water, earth, fire, and various substances believed to possess inherent powers. The verbal recitation of hymns activated and directed these material powers toward specific goals.

Timing held crucial importance, with certain spells requiring performance at specific hours, lunar phases, or seasonal moments. This temporal precision reflects Vedic cosmological thinking wherein cosmic rhythms directly influenced terrestrial events and magical efficacy.

The performer's state - including ritual purity, fasting, specific bodily orientations, and proper mental focus - affected the spell's potency. These requirements created a comprehensive system linking verbal formulation, material substances, temporal precision, and performer's condition into integrated magical technology.

## Deities and Supernatural Beings

While the Atharvaveda acknowledges the major Vedic deities (Indra, Agni, Varuna, Soma), it emphasizes different divine figures and introduces supernatural beings absent or marginal in other Vedas.

### Prominent Deities

**Kala** (Time) receives special attention in Book 19, which elaborates Time as cosmic principle underlying all existence. These Time hymns represent sophisticated cosmological thinking wherein temporal process constitutes ultimate reality.

**Brahmanaspati/Brihaspati** appears frequently as lord of sacred speech and magical formulation, reflecting the Atharvaveda's concern with verbal power and its proper application.

**The Ashvins**, divine physician twins, receive invocation in healing contexts, their mythological role as rescuers and healers making them natural patrons of Atharvan medical practice.

**Rohita** (the Red One), likely a solar deity, appears in cosmogonic contexts and receives extended hymns in Book 13, where he represents generative cosmic power.

### Demons and Malevolent Forces

The Atharvaveda extensively catalogs demonic beings and malevolent forces threatening human welfare:

**Rakshasas**: Demons causing disease, possessing humans, and disrupting sacrifices. Protection spells frequently invoke divine powers to repel these beings.

**Yakshas**: Ambivalent supernatural beings who might harm or help humans depending on propitiation and circumstance.

**Pisachas**: Flesh-eating demons associated with cremation grounds and corpses, representing death pollution and supernatural threat.

**Disease Demons**: Personified illnesses including fever (Takman), consumption, and other ailments appear as autonomous entities requiring exorcism or propitiation.

This demonological dimension reflects Vedic cosmology wherein supernatural beings populated the visible and invisible worlds, directly intervening in human affairs and requiring magical management.

## Medical Knowledge

The Atharvaveda constitutes one of humanity's oldest medical texts, preserving knowledge that influenced the development of Ayurveda, India's classical medical system. The text demonstrates sophisticated understanding of disease, healing, and the medicinal properties of plants.

### Medicinal Plants

Numerous hymns identify specific plants and their therapeutic applications. While botanical identification remains challenging due to changed nomenclature and ecological shifts, the text clearly distinguishes hundreds of plant species by name, appearance, and medicinal use.

The sacred plant **oshadhi** (herb) receives personification and worship as healing divinity. Herbs are invoked collectively and individually, their powers attributed to divine origin and cosmic connection. This sacred botanology integrated empirical plant knowledge with theological framework.

### Disease Concepts

The Atharvaveda recognizes multiple disease etiologies: demonic possession, divine punishment, sorcerous attack, natural imbalance, and karmic consequences. Different etiologies required different treatments, creating a pluralistic medical system.

Anatomical knowledge, while limited by lack of dissection, demonstrates awareness of major organs, bodily systems, and their functional relationships. Terms for heart, liver, kidneys, and other organs appear in healing contexts with some understanding of their physiological roles.

### Healing Methods

Treatments combined verbal formulae (mantras), medicinal substances (herbs, water, earth), ritual gestures, and divine invocation. This integrative approach reflected Vedic worldview wherein material and spiritual dimensions interpenetrated, requiring comprehensive therapeutic intervention.

The text describes various application methods: oral consumption, external application, fumigation, bathing, and binding. These practical instructions demonstrate empirical experimentation complementing magical theory.

## Philosophical Contributions

Beyond practical magic and medicine, the Atharvaveda contains significant philosophical material that influenced Upanishadic thought and later Indian philosophy.

### Cosmogonic Speculation

Several hymns address creation's origins through concepts like **Skambha** (Cosmic Pillar or Support) in Book 10, which describes reality's fundamental structure supporting all existence. This cosmology influenced later philosophical frameworks in Vedanta and Samkhya.

The **Prithivi Sukta** (Hymn to Earth) in Book 12 represents sophisticated ecological and cosmological thinking, celebrating the earth as maternal source, supporting foundation, and comprehensive container of all beings. This hymn influenced later environmental ethics in Hindu thought.

### Brahman Concept

The Atharvaveda contributes significantly to developing the concept of **brahman** as ultimate reality underlying phenomenal existence. Several philosophical hymns explore brahman's nature, relationship to the cosmos, and connection to ritual knowledge, anticipating Upanishadic non-dualism.

### Theory of Correspondences

The text elaborates correspondences between microcosm (human body and individual) and macrocosm (universe and cosmic principles). These correlations informed ritual practice, medical theory, and philosophical anthropology, establishing frameworks that pervade subsequent Indian thought.

## Historical Development and Canonical Status

The Atharvaveda's path to canonical recognition was complex and contested. Early Vedic tradition recognized only three Vedas (trayi vidya), with the Atharvaveda excluded from this authoritative collection. Suspicion toward the Atharvaveda reflected orthodox concerns about its magical content, folk origins, and deviation from purely sacrificial focus.

Gradually, however, the Atharvaveda gained recognition as the fourth Veda, a status firmly established by the late Vedic period. This acceptance reflected both the text's practical utility and the successful integration of Atharvan priests into the orthodox Vedic establishment through the role of Brahma priest in shrauta sacrifices.

The name "Atharvaveda" derives from **Atharvan**, a legendary priestly family or individual rishi credited with discovering fire and establishing its ritual use. This etymology links the text to fundamental Vedic concerns while distinguishing it from the Rigvedic tradition.

## Translation History and Griffith's Achievement

Before Griffith's complete translation, Western knowledge of the Atharvaveda remained fragmentary. Earlier scholars including Roth and Whitney had translated selections, but comprehensive English translation awaited Griffith's effort in the 1890s.

Griffith's translation followed his characteristic approach: reliance on traditional commentaries (particularly Sayana), attempt at metrical rendering where possible, and prioritization of readability. The Atharvaveda's often prosaic magical instructions challenged metrical translation more than the Rigveda's elevated poetry, leading to mixed results in poetic quality.

Contemporary scholars recognize Griffith's translation as pioneering but acknowledge its limitations. Whitney's partial translation (1905) provided more literal renderings with extensive philological notes, while modern translations by Bloomfield, Griffith, and others incorporate linguistic and comparative evidence unavailable to 19th-century scholars.

Nevertheless, Griffith's complete translation made the entire Atharvaveda accessible to English readers for the first time, influencing both scholarly study and popular reception of Vedic literature. His work documented the state of Atharvan studies in the late 19th century and preserved interpretive traditions valuable for historians of scholarship.

## Comparative Context

The Atharvaveda's combination of practical magic, medicine, and philosophy parallels traditions in other ancient cultures. Greek magical papyri, Mesopotamian incantations, Germanic charms, and Celtic healing traditions exhibit similar integration of verbal formulae, material substances, and supernatural invocation.

Within the Indo-European context, the Atharvaveda preserves elements likely inherited from Proto-Indo-European magical and medical traditions. Comparative analysis reveals parallels in healing formulas, protection spells, and cosmological concepts across Indo-European cultures, suggesting shared heritage predating the Indo-Aryan migration into India.

The text's folk medicine anticipates and influenced Ayurveda's development, with Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita building upon Atharvan foundations while systematizing medical knowledge into comprehensive theoretical frameworks. The relationship between Vedic folk healing and classical Ayurveda represents important continuity in Indian medical history.

## Digital Access and Contemporary Relevance

Griffith's Atharvaveda translation resides in the public domain, freely accessible through multiple digital repositories:

**Sacred Texts Archive** (sacred-texts.com) presents the complete translation in web-friendly format with search capabilities and cross-referencing.

**Internet Archive** hosts scanned volumes of the original 1895-1896 publication, preserving Griffith's text with introductions and scholarly apparatus.

**Global Grey Ebooks** and similar services offer free downloads in various digital formats compatible with modern reading devices.

These digital resources ensure continued accessibility to this distinctive Vedic text for students, scholars, practitioners of traditional healing systems, historians of religion, and general readers interested in ancient Indian culture. The Atharvaveda's practical orientation makes it particularly engaging for audiences seeking insight into daily life in Vedic society beyond the elite ritual contexts dominating other Vedic texts.
