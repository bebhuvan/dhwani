# History of Indian and Eastern Architecture

## Overview

The *History of Indian and Eastern Architecture* first appeared in 1876 as James Fergusson's comprehensive synthesis of Indian architectural traditions, establishing the foundational taxonomy for the field. Following Fergusson's death in 1886, the work was substantially revised and published in two volumes in 1910 by James Burgess (1832-1916), former Director of the Archaeological Survey of India, and Richard Phené Spiers (1838-1916), a noted architect and architectural historian. The 1910 edition, comprising approximately 971 pages across two volumes with nearly 300 maps, plans, and drawings, represented the most thorough survey of South Asian and Eastern architecture published in the early twentieth century.

Fergusson's work emerged from a decade of travel and documentation in India (1829-1839), during which he systematically recorded monuments using a draughtsman's pad, camera lucida, and diary—methodologies subsequently adopted by the Department of Curation of Ancient Monuments for survey projects in the 1880s. The 1876 original positioned itself as the first comprehensive history of Indian architecture, organizing the subcontinent's monuments into regional, religious, and chronological categories. Unlike earlier picturesque approaches favored by artists such as Thomas Daniell and William Hodges, Fergusson emphasized visual accuracy and empirical documentation, treating architecture as a decipherable text capable of revealing India's historical chronology through structural analysis.

The work coincided with critical developments in colonial knowledge production. The Archaeological Survey of India, established in 1861 through efforts by Fergusson and British archaeologists, provided institutional support for systematic monument documentation. Simultaneously, advances in photography offered unprecedented precision for recording architectural details, a technology Fergusson championed as "the most accurate means of capturing that legible history for future study." The 1876 publication thus synthesized emerging photographic documentation with traditional architectural drawing, creating what became the standard reference work for Indian architectural studies for several decades.

## About the Author — James Fergusson

James Fergusson (1808-1886) was born in Ayr, Scotland, the son of William Fergusson, an army surgeon and medical writer. After attending the Royal High School in Edinburgh and a private school in Hounslow, Fergusson departed for India without formal architectural training. He worked as a manager at Fairlie, Fergusson & Co. in Calcutta, successfully operating an indigo factory that enabled his early retirement and relocation to London to pursue architectural scholarship full-time.

Fergusson's architectural career began with *The Rock-cut Temples of India* (1845), followed by *The Illustrated Handbook of Architecture* (1855) and the monumental *A History of Architecture in All Countries from the Earliest Times to the Present Day* (1865-67). His *Tree and Serpent Worship* (1868) analyzed Buddhist remains at Sanchi and Amaravati, incorporating fifty-seven albumen print photographs—thirty-seven by William H. Griggs (1854) and twenty by James S. Waterhouse (1866)—making it an outstanding early photographic study of Indian monuments. In 1871, Fergusson received the Royal Gold Medal from the Royal Institute of British Architects, recognizing his comparative historical approach to global architectural studies.

Fergusson's methodology derived from Scottish Enlightenment empiricism and orientalist scholarship, particularly James Prinsep's epigraphic work, which Fergusson credited with inspiring his decision to bring "architecture within the domain of science." He developed a classificatory system distinguishing "true styles"—architecture emerging organically from rational building practices using local materials and labor traditions—from "false styles" that merely copied historical forms. Fergusson identified Indian architecture and Gothic medieval construction as exemplars of "true" principles, arguing that illiterate Indian craftsmen transmitted authentic architectural knowledge orally, producing work superior to Western designs dependent on copied patterns from architectural publications. However, his theoretical framework incorporated problematic evolutionary and racial taxonomies, differentiating architectural development along "Aryan"/"Dravidian" and "Sanskritic"/"Non-Sanskritic" trajectories.

## The Work

**Scope and Methodology:**

Volume I of the 1910 edition addresses Buddhist architecture, including the rock-cut cave temples at Ajanta and Ellora, alongside early structural monuments such as the stupas at Sanchi and Amaravati. Fergusson's typological analysis of Buddhist caves attempted to establish relative chronology through structural comparison, though he acknowledged in an appendix "On Some Disputed Points of Indian Chronology" that "epochal dates can hardly be attached either to the groups or to individual caves without the risk of their being upset by subsequent investigations." The volume proceeds to Dravidian architecture of southern India, examining Chola temple complexes, and the later Chalukyan style from southern and central India during the early medieval period.

Volume II encompasses Jain architecture, Indo-Aryan (northern Hindu) temple traditions including sites such as Khajuraho and the Konark Sun Temple, and Islamic architecture in India spanning the Delhi Sultanate and Mughal periods. Fergusson established five primary taxonomic categories: Buddhist, northern Hindu, Jaina, southern (Tamil) Hindu, and "modern Hindu" (synthesizing northern Hindu and Islamic elements). The volume concludes with architectural traditions of Burma, Cambodia, Thailand, Java, China, and Japan, positioning Indian architectural principles within broader Asian contexts.

Fergusson's analytical framework emphasized structural logic, proposing that Indian architecture followed a consistent process wherein structures were completed before receiving sculptural and decorative embellishment. His chronological classifications relied on correlating undated monuments with securely dated examples through stylistic comparison—a methodology he pioneered as "the first among art historians to have studied Indian Architecture by analysing the style and distinct characteristics of individual groups of buildings and correlating these to dated examples."

**Historical Context:**

The *History of Indian and Eastern Architecture* emerged during the formative period of systematic archaeological documentation in British India. The establishment of the Archaeological Survey in 1861 provided institutional infrastructure for monument preservation and study, while photographic technology offered new possibilities for accurate visual recording. Fergusson positioned his work at the intersection of these developments, arguing that "advancements in archaeology and photography have greatly enriched our understanding of Indian architectural history."

The work reflected Victorian architectural education's search for design principles applicable to contemporary practice. Fergusson's distinction between "true" and "false" architectural styles engaged ongoing debates about appropriate models for colonial and metropolitan building, critiquing Western colonial structures in India as "exotic" impositions lacking organic relationship to local contexts. His comparative framework, linking Indian temple construction to medieval European cathedral building, sought to establish universal architectural principles transcending regional traditions while simultaneously asserting hierarchies of cultural achievement.

## Significance

**Contemporary Reception:**

The *History of Indian and Eastern Architecture* became the standard reference work for Indian architectural studies, shaping colonial architectural education and influencing building practices in British India. Fergusson's classificatory system—organizing monuments by region, religion, chronology, and ethnic attribution—provided the taxonomic framework adopted by subsequent archaeological surveys and scholarly publications. The work's integration of photographic documentation with architectural drawing established methodological precedents for field survey practices, with Fergusson's techniques becoming standard procedure for the Department of Curation of Ancient Monuments by the 1880s.

The book circulated widely in Britain, America, India, and Australia, establishing Fergusson as the preeminent authority on Indian architectural history for several decades. His emphasis on systematic visual documentation and typological analysis contributed to architecture's emergence as a formalized academic discipline, distinct from earlier antiquarian or picturesque approaches.

**Later Assessment:**

Subsequent archaeological research, particularly through advances in epigraphic dating and refined stratigraphic methodologies, revised numerous chronological determinations proposed by Fergusson. His evolutionary framework, positing that "decline characterized religions and art" and that Buddhism "succumbed to the corrupt and superstitious faith of Hinduism, which is evident in its overly ornate and decadent sculptures and buildings," reflected Victorian racial and religious prejudices that later scholarship rejected. Fergusson's theory that Amaravati sculptures represented "the culminating point attained by the art in India" due to Indo-Greek influence, with artistic decline following the diminution of Greek cultural presence, similarly exemplified problematic assumptions about Western civilization's superiority.

His 1884 publication "Archaeology in India, with Special Reference to the Works of Babu Rajendralal Mitra" exposed tensions between Fergusson's scholarship and emerging Indian archaeological voices, revealing his conviction that Indian architectural forms remained fundamentally inferior to European Classical architecture. These theoretical limitations increasingly marginalized aspects of Fergusson's interpretive framework throughout the twentieth century.

**Value for Researchers:**

Despite chronological revisions and rejection of Fergusson's evolutionary assumptions, the *History of Indian and Eastern Architecture* retains substantial documentary value. The work's comprehensive photographic and drawn records preserve visual evidence of monuments subsequently damaged or destroyed, providing critical documentation for conservation and reconstruction efforts. Fergusson's systematic surveys established baseline descriptions for numerous sites, enabling comparison with contemporary conditions to assess deterioration and guide preservation priorities.

The 1910 revision by Burgess and Spiers incorporated corrections and additions reflecting early twentieth-century archaeological advances, making it a transitional text documenting the evolution of Indian architectural studies from Victorian amateur scholarship toward professionalized archaeological practice. For historians of architectural historiography, the work exemplifies nineteenth-century approaches to classification, documentation, and interpretation, revealing the epistemological frameworks shaping colonial knowledge production about South Asian material culture.
