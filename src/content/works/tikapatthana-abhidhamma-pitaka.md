# Tikapatthana of the Abhidhamma Pitaka

## Overview

The Patthana (Pali: Paṭṭhāna, "Causal Conditions" or "Activations") constitutes the seventh and final book of the Abhidhamma Pitaka, the third division of the Theravada Buddhist Pali Canon. This monumental text represents the culmination of Abhidhamma scholastic analysis, systematically examining causal conditioning through twenty-four types of conditional relations (paccaya) applied to the classificatory matrices (matika) established in the Dhammasangani, the first Abhidhamma book. The work's title derives from its treatment of the Tika section—twenty-two triadic classifications of mental and physical phenomena—though it encompasses both Tika (triads) and Duka (dyads, numbering one hundred) groupings. Traditional Theravada accounts attribute the Abhidhamma to the Buddha himself, who purportedly conceived it immediately following his enlightenment and taught it to deities in the Tavatimsa heaven before transmitting it through Sariputta to human disciples. Modern scholarship, however, dates the Abhidhamma Pitaka's composition to approximately the third century BCE, placing it one to two centuries after the Buddha's death and attributing its development to disciples and scholastic communities rather than to the historical Buddha.

The Patthana extends beyond simple causal analysis to construct a comprehensive philosophical system explaining how all conditioned phenomena interrelate. Its central thesis maintains that apart from nibbana (nirvana), which exists as an unconditioned absolute, all phenomena arise through dependent relationships rather than divine creation or substantial essence. This framework elaborates the Buddhist doctrine of dependent origination (paticca-samuppada) through rigorous categorical methodology, demonstrating how mental states, physical processes, and material elements condition one another across multiple intersecting causal pathways. The text's analytical precision distinguishes Abhidhamma scholasticism from earlier Buddhist discourse styles, transforming practical teachings into systematic philosophical inquiry through exhaustive permutation and classification.

## About the Text

The question of the Patthana's authorship reflects broader debates concerning Abhidhamma origins. Theravada tradition preserves accounts in which the Buddha's contemplation of the Patthana caused six-colored rays to emanate from his body, symbolizing the teaching's profundity and his omniscient comprehension of universal causality. This traditional attribution served to authenticate the Abhidhamma as Buddha-vacana (word of the Buddha) and establish its canonical authority alongside the Sutta and Vinaya Pitakas. Buddhaghosa, the fifth-century CE South Indian exegete who relocated to Sri Lanka, did not compose the Patthana itself but authored extensive commentaries (atthakatha) explicating its technical terminology and philosophical implications. The 1921 Pali Text Society edition edited by Caroline Augusta Foley Rhys Davids represents scholarly efforts to produce critical editions of Abhidhamma texts for Western academic study.

The Patthana's scale defies easy comprehension: it spans over 6,000 pages in the Siamese (Thai) printed edition and comprises five substantial volumes in Burmese recensions. This vast extent results from its systematic methodology, which applies twenty-four conditional relations across multiple classificatory frameworks through four major analytical divisions and six methodological applications, generating twenty-four distinct treatises collectively termed the Mahapakarana ("Great Treatise"). The commentary and sub-commentary traditions designate it "Anantanaya Samanta Patthana" ("The Patthana of Infinite Methods and Universal Relations"), acknowledging its fathomless analytical depth. Despite its abstract tabular format and repetitive structure, scholars recognize the Patthana as "one of the truly monumental products of the human mind, astounding in its breadth of vision, its rigorous consistency, and its painstaking attention to detail."

## The Work

The Patthana's analytical framework rests on twenty-four paccayas (conditional relations), which enumerate all possible ways one phenomenon may function in conditioning another's arising, persistence, or cessation. These relations comprise: (1) hetu-paccaya (root cause), (2) arammana-paccaya (object support), (3) adhipati-paccaya (predominance), (4) anantara-paccaya (proximity), (5) samanantara-paccaya (contiguity), (6) sahajata-paccaya (conascence/simultaneity), (7) aññamañña-paccaya (reciprocity/mutuality), (8) nissaya-paccaya (support/dependence), (9) upanissaya-paccaya (decisive support), (10) purejata-paccaya (prenascence/pre-existence), (11) pacchajata-paccaya (postnascence/post-existence), (12) asevana-paccaya (repetition/habitual cultivation), (13) kamma-paccaya (karma), (14) vipaka-paccaya (consequence/fruition), (15) ahara-paccaya (nutriment), (16) indriya-paccaya (controlling faculty), (17) jhana-paccaya (meditative absorption), (18) magga-paccaya (path), (19) sampayutta-paccaya (association), (20) vippayutta-paccaya (dissociation), (21) atthi-paccaya (presence), (22) natthi-paccaya (absence), (23) vigata-paccaya (disappearance), and (24) avigata-paccaya (non-disappearance). While comprehensive, these twenty-four can be reduced to four fundamental conditions—object, decisive support, karma, and presence—demonstrating both analytical exhaustiveness and theoretical parsimony.

The text's architecture comprises four major divisions (patthana) examining conditional relations from complementary perspectives. The Anuloma Patthana analyzes instances where specified paccaya relations exist between dharmas (phenomena). The Paccaniya Patthana studies cases where such relations do not obtain. The Anuloma-Paccaniya Patthana investigates mixed scenarios where some conditional relations exist while others do not. The Paccaniya-Anuloma Patthana examines the inverse: situations where some relations are absent while others obtain. Each division applies the twenty-four paccayas through six methodological approaches (involving permutations of the Tika and Duka classificatory groups), producing twenty-four treatises that systematically exhaust all possible relational configurations between conditioned phenomena.

The Patthana applies its conditional framework to philosophical analysis of dependent origination, demonstrating how mental formations, consciousness, name-and-form, sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, clinging, becoming, birth, and death interconnect through multiple simultaneous causal relationships rather than simple linear sequences. This multi-dimensional analysis employs matika (matrix) structures—detailed taxonomic lists organizing phenomena by ethical quality (kusala/akusala/abyakata), by feeling-tone (pleasant/painful/neutral), and by relationship to results. The technical terminology includes specialized concepts like sahajata (co-arising phenomena that condition each other simultaneously), purejata (antecedent conditions from prior moments), and pacchajata (subsequent conditions that retroactively support earlier states). This rigorous categorical apparatus enables precise philosophical distinctions unavailable in earlier Buddhist literature, establishing patterns that influenced both Theravada and Sarvastivada Abhidharma traditions.

## Historical Significance

The Patthana occupies a central position in Theravada Abhidhamma tradition, representing the system's most sophisticated analytical achievement. Its completion marks the full development of Buddhist scholastic methodology, transforming practical meditation instructions and ethical guidelines into comprehensive philosophical frameworks addressing metaphysics, epistemology, and psychology. The text's influence extends throughout Theravada intellectual history, as commentarial literature by Buddhaghosa and later scholars presupposes familiarity with Patthana concepts and terminology. Medieval Theravada philosophical debates concerning momentariness (khanika-vada), the nature of nibbana, and the relationship between ultimate realities (paramattha-dhamma) and conventional truths (sammuti) drew extensively on Patthana analysis.

Scholarly study of the Patthana traditionally requires extensive preliminary training in Abhidhamma thought. Students typically master the Abhidhammattha-sangaha, a medieval compendium systematizing Abhidhamma doctrine, before approaching original canonical texts. The Patthana's length, abstract style, and technical complexity have limited its accessibility even within monastic education systems, though reformist movements in Burma (Myanmar) during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries emphasized Abhidhamma study as demonstrating Buddhist philosophical rigor. In Burmese Buddhism particularly, the five volumes of the Patthana function as powerful parittas (protective texts): ritualized chanting of the complete text, often performed over days or weeks, purportedly wards off dangers and safeguards Buddhist teachings. This protective function symbolizes the Patthana as embodying the Buddha's omniscience and the Dhamma's comprehensiveness.

The text's influence on broader Buddhist philosophy manifests in both adoption and critique. Madhyamaka philosophers like Nagarjuna, while rejecting Abhidharma essentialism, engaged with conditional analysis frameworks when articulating emptiness (shunyata) doctrine. Yogacara thinkers incorporated modified versions of conditional relations theory into their consciousness-based metaphysics. Modern scholarly engagement includes Venerable U Narada's (Mulapatthana Sayadaw) English translation of the first two volumes under the title "Conditional Relations" (Pali Text Society, 1969, 1981), making portions accessible to non-Pali readers. Contemporary philosophers of mind have noted structural parallels between Patthana's analysis of mental event conditioning and modern theories of neural network causation, though such comparisons require careful methodological qualification given fundamentally different explanatory frameworks.
