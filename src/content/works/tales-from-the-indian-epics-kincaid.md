## Historical Context

Published in 1918, the work systematically adapts episodic narratives from foundational Sanskrit texts—specifically the Mahabharata, Ramayana, and Bhagavata Purana—into accessible English prose designed for child readers and Western audiences. # Tales from the Indian Epics

## Overview

Published in 1918 by Oxford University Press in Bombay, *Tales from the Indian Epics* represents Charles Augustus Kincaid's deliberate effort to make subsidiary narratives from Sanskrit epic literature accessible to English-speaking audiences, particularly children within the colonial education system. The 148-page volume, illustrated by prominent Bombay School artist M.V. Dhurandhar (1867-1944), presents ten episodic tales extracted from the *Mahabharata*, *Ramayana*, and *Bhagavata Purana*. Kincaid explicitly positioned this work as a companion to his earlier *Indian Heroes* (1915), which had summarized the central heroic narratives; this subsequent collection instead focused on "stories which are incidentally related in the Ramayana and Mahabharata," thereby introducing English readers to the nested narrative structure characteristic of Sanskrit epic tradition. The work appeared at the height of British imperial power in India, published the same year as Kincaid's first volume of *A History of the Maratha People*, demonstrating his dual engagement with both popular and scholarly mediation of Indian cultural materials for English-language markets.

The work emerged during a pivotal period in British India when Oxford University Press's Bombay branch, established in 1912, actively pursued publication of Indian classical materials for both educational and orientalist purposes. OUP Bombay's establishment followed the Press's great transformation in the 1860s from a learned and religious publisher to an institution interpreting eastern civilizations for western audiences, heavily influenced by Max Müller's fifty-volume *Sacred Books of the East* project. Kincaid's retellings served dual colonial functions: providing British and Anglo-Indian children with sanitized versions of Indian mythology while simultaneously making these narratives available to English-educated Indian children distanced from Sanskrit sources by caste restrictions, linguistic barriers, or anglicized education. The selection of Dhurandhar as illustrator—a highly regarded Indian artist often compared to Raja Ravi Varma and a founder of The Art Society of India (1918)—represented a conscious integration of Indian artistic sensibility with colonial publishing infrastructure, though filtered through academic training at Bombay's Sir Jamsetjee Jeejeebhoy School of Art. Dhurandhar's prolific career included over 5,000 paintings and 50,000 illustrations for various colonial publications, making him the preeminent visual interpreter of Indian subjects for English-language audiences.

Kincaid's narrative methodology involved substantial simplification and moral framing aligned with Victorian children's literature conventions, excising elements deemed unsuitable for young readers while emphasizing themes of duty, devotion, and supernatural intervention. His prose employed the characteristic register of late imperial didactic literature: clear, authoritative, morally unambiguous, designed to instruct as much as to entertain. The book's distribution through Oxford University Press's educational networks ensured its circulation in colonial schools throughout India and Britain, establishing it as a formative text for generations of readers encountering Indian epic literature through English-language mediation rather than vernacular oral or textual traditions. Published at twelve shillings sixpence, the volume occupied a middle-class market position, affordable for colonial administrative families and missionary institutions but priced beyond most Indian readers without institutional access.

## About the Author — Charles Augustus Kincaid

Charles Augustus Kincaid, CVO (1870-1954) served as a high court judge and senior administrator in the Indian Civil Service, son of Major-General William Kincaid, formerly Resident of Bhopal. Born in Indore in February 1870, Kincaid received his secondary education at Sherborne School before entering the ICS, spending his entire professional career in British India's administrative and judicial apparatus. He developed extensive expertise in Marathi history and culture that distinguished him from many colonial officials who remained culturally detached from the regions they governed. His most substantial scholarly achievement was the three-volume *A History of the Maratha People* (1918-1925), co-authored with Rao Bahadur Dattatray Balwant Parasnis, which drew on original Maratha documents, various *bakhars*, and the *Shivdigvijay* to present a sympathetic account of Shivaji and Maratha political history. This work provoked objections from senior British officials precisely because of its favorable portrayal of Indian resistance to Mughal and British power, revealing Kincaid's complex position as a colonial administrator who developed genuine scholarly engagement with Maharashtrian history while simultaneously serving the imperial system that subjugated it. The collaboration with Parasnis, himself an accomplished Marathi historian, demonstrated Kincaid's willingness to work with Indian scholars on relatively equal intellectual terms—unusual for colonial administrators of his generation.

Kincaid proved extraordinarily prolific, authoring numerous works that mediated Indian cultural materials for English audiences, including *Deccan Nursery Tales*, *Tales of Pandharpur*, and *Tales of King Vikrama*. His children's literature represented a distinct strand of colonial publishing that sought to incorporate Indian mythological and folkloric traditions into educational frameworks dominated by British literary models. Unlike purely orientalist scholarship aimed at academic audiences—such as the detailed philological work produced by European Sanskritists—Kincaid's retellings targeted pedagogical markets, functioning as textbooks and leisure reading within colonial educational institutions throughout India, Burma, and Britain itself. His work occupied the expanding market for colonial children's literature that presented the empire as knowable, manageable space where British children could encounter cultural difference through safely domesticated narratives. *Indian Heroes* achieved particular popularity, becoming Kincaid's most widely circulated work and establishing his reputation as interpreter of Indian mythology for juvenile audiences. His son, Dennis Kincaid, continued this engagement with Indian cultural history, authoring *British Social Life in India, 1608-1937* (1938), which documented the social dimensions of colonial presence with attention to the everyday practices that sustained British communities in India across three centuries.

Kincaid's literary output reflects the contradictions of liberal imperialism: genuine appreciation for Indian cultural traditions combined with unquestioned assumptions about British civilizational superiority and the necessity of English-language mediation. His award of Commander of the Royal Victorian Order recognized his service to the empire, situating his scholarly work within the framework of colonial administration rather than independent academic inquiry. His works remain significant as artifacts of colonial knowledge production, documenting both the transmission pathways through which Indian classical literature reached twentieth-century English readers and the interpretive filters through which that transmission occurred. Contemporary postcolonial scholarship examines Kincaid's retellings as examples of how imperial power operated through cultural translation, making colonized traditions legible to metropolitan audiences while simultaneously asserting European epistemological authority over indigenous knowledge systems.

## The Work

*Tales from the Indian Epics* comprises ten narratives drawn primarily from the *Adi Parva* and *Vana Parva* sections of the *Mahabharata*, with "The Descent of the Ganges" adapted from the *Bala Kanda* of the *Ramayana* and "The Tale of the Pole Star" from the *Bhagavata Purana*. The selected stories include cosmogenic myths ("The Churning of the Ocean"), sacrificial legends ("King Janamejaya's Snake Sacrifice"), romance narratives ("Nala and Damayanti," "Satyavan and Savitri," "Ruru and Pramadvara"), didactic tales ("Veda's Pupil," "Vrigu and Agni"), and transformation myths ("The Frog King's Daughter"). This selection reveals Kincaid's pedagogical priorities: emphasizing narratives with clear moral lessons, romantic devotion, and supernatural intervention while avoiding the more politically charged or philosophically complex episodes found in the epics' main storylines. Notably absent are narratives concerning caste hierarchy, political intrigue, or the problematic treatment of women found throughout the Sanskrit texts—elements that might have troubled Victorian sensibilities or raised uncomfortable questions about Hindu social structures. The heavy emphasis on romance narratives like "Nala and Damayanti" and "Satyavan and Savitri" aligned with colonial expectations that Indian literature was primarily romantic and devotional rather than political or philosophical.

Kincaid's adaptation methodology involved radical compression and simplification of Sanskrit originals, removing nested narrative frames, condensing elaborate descriptions, and eliminating elements deemed inappropriate for child readers—including explicit sexuality, complex theological debates, and graphic violence. His prose style follows late Victorian children's literature conventions, employing straightforward declarative sentences, simplified dialogue, and explicit moral commentary absent from Sanskrit sources. Where the *Mahabharata* and *Ramayana* employ sophisticated literary techniques including multiple perspectives, narrative recursion, and philosophical ambiguity, Kincaid's retellings present single-perspective narratives with unambiguous moral valences. The Sanskrit epics' characteristic verse form disappears entirely, replaced by prosaic English narrative that erases the metrical sophistication of the originals. Kincaid's translations typically worked from existing English versions rather than Sanskrit originals—he cites no specific Sanskrit editions in his preface, suggesting reliance on earlier translations by scholars with stronger philological credentials. This secondary translation process introduced additional layers of mediation between Sanskrit texts and English child readers.

The inclusion of Dhurandhar's six illustrations provided visual interpretation filtered through academic naturalism rather than traditional Indian iconographic conventions. Dhurandhar's training at the Sir Jamsetjee Jeejeebhoy School of Art emphasized European techniques of perspective, anatomy, and composition, producing images that mediated between Western artistic expectations and Indian subject matter. His illustrations for Kincaid's volume employed realistic figuration, dramatic lighting, and compositional techniques derived from European academic painting rather than the stylized forms, symbolic coloring, and hieratic arrangements characteristic of traditional Indian manuscript illustration or popular religious chromolithography. This visual-textual collaboration exemplified the hybridized cultural production characteristic of colonial Bombay's artistic and publishing networks, where Indian artists trained in European techniques produced images of Indian subjects for consumption by anglicized audiences. Dhurandhar's work represented what art historians have termed the "Bombay School"—artists who navigated between indigenous iconographic traditions and colonial artistic pedagogy, creating works that satisfied neither traditional religious markets nor metropolitan aesthetic standards but found acceptance in colonial educational and commercial contexts.

Kincaid's preface explicitly positions the work as supplementary to *Indian Heroes*, suggesting an intended readership already familiar with the epics' primary narratives—likely students in colonial schools where Indian literature featured as component of curricula designed to produce culturally literate colonial subjects capable of administering India's diverse populations. The book's modest length and accessible prose style facilitated its use as both classroom text and domestic leisure reading, circulating through institutional and private libraries throughout British India and the metropole. Its publication by Oxford University Press rather than missionary presses or commercial publishers gave it scholarly authority and educational legitimacy, ensuring adoption in government schools, missionary institutions, and private Anglo-Indian academies. The book participated in colonial education's contradictory project: exposing students to Indian cultural materials while containing those materials within frameworks that reaffirmed British civilizational superiority.

## Historical Significance

*Tales from the Indian Epics* occupied an important niche in early twentieth-century English-language transmission of Sanskrit epic literature, serving as accessible introduction for readers lacking direct access to vernacular traditions or scholarly translations. Published during World War I when British India contributed massive military and economic resources to the imperial war effort, the book's circulation coincided with intensifying debates about colonial education, Indian cultural autonomy, and the role of English-language instruction in maintaining imperial control. The year 1918 witnessed both the final stages of the Great War and increasing nationalist agitation in India, including protests against the Rowlatt Act proposals that would culminate in the 1919 Jallianwala Bagh massacre. Kincaid's retellings participated in what scholars have termed "colonial domestication" of Indian classical literature—making these traditions legible within British literary and pedagogical frameworks while obscuring their original cultural contexts and religious significance. The book appeared alongside other colonial cultural products designed to represent India to both British and anglicized Indian audiences, including archaeological surveys, museum exhibitions, and orientalist scholarship that constructed India as ancient, spiritual, and requiring European mediation for proper understanding.

The work influenced subsequent generations of English-language epic retellings, establishing narrative patterns and selective emphases that persisted throughout the twentieth century. Its focus on romantic and devotional episodes rather than martial or political narratives shaped popular Western conceptions of Indian mythology as primarily concerned with supernatural love stories and moral exempla rather than complex explorations of *dharma*, statecraft, and cosmic order. This selective transmission had lasting consequences for how Indian epic literature circulated in global Anglophone literary markets, privileging certain narrative types while marginalizing others. Later twentieth-century retellings by authors including R.K. Narayan, William Buck, and contemporary writers like Chitra Banerjee Divakaruni and Devdutt Pattanaik continued patterns established by colonial-era adaptations like Kincaid's, though with varying degrees of critical engagement with those precedents. The persistence of romance-focused, morally simplified epic narratives in English-language markets can be traced partially to formative texts like *Tales from the Indian Epics* that established reader expectations and publisher preferences for how Sanskrit materials should appear in translation.

Within colonial education systems, books like Kincaid's functioned as tools for creating bicultural colonial subjects—Indians educated in English who could navigate both British administrative culture and Indian social contexts, yet whose understanding of their own classical traditions was mediated through colonial interpretive frameworks. The availability of such texts simultaneously made Sanskrit literature accessible to Indians barred from traditional *brahmanical* education systems while imposing Victorian moral and literary sensibilities on materials originally composed within radically different cultural contexts. This double operation—democratizing access while controlling interpretation—exemplifies the complex cultural politics of colonial knowledge production. For lower-caste Indians and women excluded from Sanskrit learning by traditional religious authorities, English-language retellings provided alternative access to epic narratives, though at the cost of accepting colonial mediation and Victorian moral frameworks. The book thus participated in both the erosion of traditional religious educational monopolies and the establishment of new forms of cultural gatekeeping operating through English literacy and colonial publishing infrastructure.

The book's republication through Internet Archive and Wikisource as part of digitization initiatives has given it renewed circulation in the twenty-first century, now read by global audiences seeking accessible introductions to Indian epic literature. Contemporary readers encounter Kincaid's work as historical artifact documenting early twentieth-century colonial literary culture rather than as current scholarly resource, its value residing in what it reveals about the mediation processes through which Indian classical literature reached English-language audiences during the colonial period.
