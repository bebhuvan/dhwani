# हिन्दुस्तानी कहावत कोश (Hindustani Kahavat Kosh)

## Overview

The *Hindustani Kahavat Kosh*, published by National Book Trust India and translated by Krishnananda Gupta, represents vernacular adaptation of S.W. Fallon's monumental "A Dictionary of Hindustani Proverbs," originally compiled during the 1870s-1880s and published in Benares in 1886. This 382-page Hindi/Devanagari edition makes accessible to contemporary readers Fallon's comprehensive documentation of approximately 12,500 proverbs, sayings, emblems, aphorisms, maxims, and similes drawn from Northern Indian vernacular languages including Hindi, Urdu, Punjabi, Marwari, Bhojpuri, Maggah (Magahi), and Tirhuti (Maithili). The work exemplifies how colonial-era linguistic documentation—conducted by British administrators and scholars with complex motivations combining scholarly interest, administrative utility, and orientalist fascination—can be recovered and disseminated for contemporary Indian audiences who constitute these linguistic traditions' native speaker communities.

Fallon's original dictionary emerged from distinctive moment in colonial linguistic scholarship when British administrators, recognizing that effective governance required understanding local languages and cultures, sponsored systematic documentation of Indian vernacular languages. Unlike earlier colonial linguistic work focusing primarily on classical languages (Sanskrit, Persian) or standardized literary vernaculars (literary Hindi, Urdu), scholars like Fallon extended attention to regional variations, colloquial speech, and folk traditions often marginalized in formal linguistic study. His interest in proverbs reflected recognition that proverbial literature encodes cultural values, social wisdom, and linguistic patterns crucial for understanding how ordinary people conceptualized and communicated about their world.

The National Book Trust edition's publication represents modern institutional commitment to preserving and disseminating India's linguistic heritage. The National Book Trust, established by Government of India in 1957 with objectives including producing quality literature in Hindi and other Indian languages at moderate prices, participates in projects making historically significant texts accessible to contemporary audiences. This Hindi edition enables readers lacking access to rare colonial-era publications or unfamiliar with nineteenth-century publishing conventions to engage this rich proverbial heritage, while serving contemporary Hindi linguistic, literary, and folklore studies through historical comparative materials.

## The Original Work: S.W. Fallon's Dictionary

S.W. Fallon worked during the 1870s-1880s as British administrator in Northern India, developing keen interest in local dialects and vernacular speech patterns. His methodological approach combined systematic collection with careful documentation: he recorded not merely proverbs' linguistic forms but contextual usage, regional variations, cultural meanings, and English translations. This ethnographic attention distinguished his work from purely philological documentation, treating proverbs as significant folklore providing insights into cultural values, social relations, and worldviews.

The dictionary's scope proved remarkably comprehensive. While centered on Hindi and Urdu—the dominant literary languages of Northern India—Fallon included substantial materials from Punjabi (spoken across Punjab region), Marwari (Rajasthani variety spoken in Marwar region), Bhojpuri (spoken across eastern Uttar Pradesh and western Bihar), Maggah/Magahi (spoken in southern Bihar), and Tirhuti/Maithili (spoken in northern Bihar and Nepal's Terai). This multilingual coverage documented linguistic diversity across Northern India's regional varieties, preserving evidence of linguistic forms and cultural expressions from period before twentieth-century standardization processes significantly transformed vernacular speech.

The original 1886 publication appeared at specific moment in Northern Indian linguistic history. The late nineteenth century witnessed intensifying debates about Hindi-Urdu distinctions, standardization of literary languages, and relationships between regional vernaculars and emerging national language politics. Colonial educational policies promoted standardized languages while often marginalizing regional varieties. Urban migration and expanding print culture were beginning to erode traditional rural speech patterns. Fallon's documentation thus captured linguistic diversity at moment of significant transformation, preserving materials that might otherwise have been lost as standardization and modernization pressures intensified.

Following Fallon's death, the work was edited and revised by R.C. Temple—himself significant figure in colonial folklore and linguistic scholarship—with assistance from Faqir Chand (Lala), indicating collaboration between British and Indian scholars in preserving and interpreting this linguistic heritage. Temple's editorial work ensured the dictionary's continued circulation and scholarly utility beyond Fallon's lifetime.

## Proverbial Literature: Linguistic and Cultural Significance

Proverbs constitute distinctive linguistic and cultural form combining concise expression with accumulated social wisdom. Unlike formal philosophical or religious texts, proverbs emerge from oral folk tradition, transmitted across generations through everyday speech, encoding culturally specific knowledge about human nature, social relations, practical wisdom, and moral values. Their study provides insights unavailable from examining only literary or formal linguistic sources.

Linguistically, proverbs demonstrate vernacular speech's creative capacities: metaphorical innovation, rhythmic patterns, memorable phrasing, and semantic compression enabling complex meanings through brief expressions. They employ dialect-specific vocabulary and grammatical forms often absent from standardized literary languages, documenting linguistic diversity within broader language families. Regional proverb variations reveal how different communities express similar concepts through locally meaningful imagery and references.

Culturally, proverbs reflect values, social norms, and worldviews of communities producing and transmitting them. Agricultural proverbs reveal farming practices and environmental knowledge. Social proverbs encode attitudes toward family relations, gender roles, caste hierarchies, and community obligations. Economic proverbs reflect market practices and commercial ethics. Political proverbs express attitudes toward authority, justice, and governance. Studying proverbial literature thus enables reconstructing cultural attitudes and social practices from historical periods where other documentation may be limited.

For Northern Indian languages documented in Fallon's dictionary, proverbial literature additionally reflects complex linguistic situation where multiple languages and varieties coexisted and interacted. Hindi-Urdu continuum's fluidity appears in shared proverbs crossing supposed linguistic boundaries. Persian and Sanskrit loan words in proverbs document these classical languages' influences on vernacular speech. Regional variations of similar proverbs illuminate how cultural concepts translate across linguistic boundaries while adapting to local contexts.

## Colonial Linguistic Scholarship: Contexts and Complexities

Fallon's work exemplifies colonial-era linguistic scholarship's complex character, combining genuine scholarly achievement with problematic power dynamics and orientalist assumptions. British administrators' interest in Indian languages served multiple purposes simultaneously: enabling more effective governance by understanding local populations; satisfying intellectual curiosity about exotic languages and cultures; demonstrating British scholarly superiority through systematic documentation; and asserting colonial authority through comprehensive knowledge production about subject populations.

Scholars like Fallon often developed genuine appreciation for Indian languages and cultures, recognizing their sophistication and valuing their preservation. Yet this appreciation operated within colonial power structures where British scholars claimed authority to document, interpret, and represent Indian traditions to both Western and Indian audiences. The very act of creating systematic dictionaries and grammars imposed Western scholarly frameworks on indigenous linguistic knowledge, potentially obscuring how native speakers themselves understood and organized their languages.

The question of who benefits from such documentation proves complex. Colonial administrators utilized linguistic knowledge for governance purposes—understanding local grievances, detecting resistance movements, administering justice through vernacular courts. Western scholars used documented materials for comparative philology and orientalist scholarship serving European intellectual agendas. Yet the documentation itself preserved linguistic materials that might otherwise have been lost, creating resources that contemporary Indian scholars and communities can utilize for their own purposes.

Post-colonial recovery of colonial-era linguistic documentation requires critical engagement: acknowledging the problematic contexts and power dynamics of original compilation while recognizing the materials' potential value for contemporary linguistic, literary, and cultural studies. Modern editions like this National Book Trust publication can make colonial-era documentation accessible to Indian audiences who constitute primary stakeholders in these linguistic traditions, enabling them to engage critically with this documentation while utilizing it for contemporary scholarly and cultural purposes.
