## Historical Context

Valmiki's Ramayana represents the seminal Sanskrit epic that fundamentally shaped classical Indian literary, philosophical, and cultural discourse, emerging during the transitional period between the Vedic and Classical Sanskrit eras (circa 7th-5th centuries BCE). Traditionally attributed to the sage Valmiki, considered the "first poet" (adi-kavi), the text comprises approximately 24,000 verses across seven narrative sections (kandas), chronicling the life, exile, and heroic journey of Prince Rama of Ayodhya. Beyond its narrative complexity, the epic articulates sophisticated philosophical principles of dharma (ethical and cosmic order), ideal kingship, familial duty, and individual moral responsibility. The work innovatively introduced the shloka metrical form and established narrative techniques that profoundly influenced subsequent literary traditions across Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain textual landscapes. Its core narrative—depicting Rama's righteous struggle against the demon-king Ravana, punctuated by his wife Sita's abduction and eventual rescue—transcends mere mythological recounting, serving as a profound allegorical exploration of human ethical challenges, divine intervention, and societal ideals. The Ramayana's intellectual and cultural significance extends far beyond its original Sanskrit composition, having been translated, reinterpreted, and performed across multiple languages and performance traditions throughout South and Southeast Asia. Its characters—particularly Rama, Sita, Hanuman, and Ravana—have become archetypes embodying complex moral and social principles, continuously reinterpreted through centuries of artistic, philosophical, and performative traditions, thereby maintaining its dynamic relevance in Indian intellectual and cultural consciousness.

## Overview

The Ramayana stands as Sanskrit literature's foundational epic, traditionally attributed to the sage-poet Valmiki and revered as the adi-kavya (first poem). Scholarly estimates place the composition of the core narrative (Books II-VI) between the 7th and 5th centuries BCE, with general consensus around 500 BCE for the oldest surviving version. The first book (Balakanda) and seventh book (Uttarakanda) represent later interpolations, added between the 2nd century BCE and 3rd century CE, evidenced by stylistic differences, narrative contradictions, and the mention of Ayodhya as Kosala's capital rather than its historical successors. The epic comprises approximately 24,000 verses organized into seven kandas (chapters) and roughly 500 sargas (sub-chapters), making it about one-quarter the length of the Mahabharata. As the adi-kavya, the Ramayana established the shloka meter and narrative conventions that shaped subsequent Indian epic poetry, making it foundational not merely chronologically but architecturally to the entire kavya tradition. The text belongs to the Itihasa genre—"narratives of past events interspersed with teachings on the goals of human life"—positioning it as simultaneously historical chronicle, moral philosophy, and aesthetic achievement.

## About Valmiki

Valmiki occupies a unique position as Adi Kavi (the first poet), revered for inventing Sanskrit literary poetry through spontaneous composition. Traditional accounts describe him as the tenth son of Varuna, though his name ("anthill-born") derives from a transformation legend: originally a thief named Lohajangha (or Ratnakar in some versions) who robbed travelers during drought, he encountered seven sages who prompted spiritual transformation through mantra meditation. Ant-hills grew around his meditating body, and upon emergence the sages blessed him with the name Valmiki. The Ramayana itself narrates how Valmiki composed the first shloka: witnessing a hunter kill one of two mating cranes (krauncha birds), grief and anger caused him to spontaneously curse the fowler in a metrically perfect couplet. The god Brahma appeared, explaining that profound emotion (shoka) had transformed into poetry (shloka), and commissioned him to compose Rama's story in this new meter. This meta-narrative establishes poetry's emotional origin while providing divine sanction for the epic. Within the Ramayana's narrative frame, Valmiki shelters the exiled Sita in his ashram, where she gives birth to twins Lava and Kusha. Valmiki teaches them the completed Ramayana, which they recite at King Rama's court, creating a recursive narrative structure where the epic describes its own composition and transmission. Modern scholarship debates historical Valmiki's existence versus composite authorship across centuries, though textual evidence suggests multiple authorial hands given stylistic variations and chronological layering.

## The Work

### Textual Structure

The Ramayana's seven kandas trace Prince Rama's life from birth through death, framing dharmic ideals through heroic narrative across approximately 24,000 verses:

- **Balakanda** (Book of Childhood, ~2,500 verses): Opens with cosmological frame and Valmiki's composition of the first shloka, then narrates King Dasharatha's yajna to obtain sons, resulting in miraculous birth of four princes—Rama, Bharata, Lakshmana, Shatrughna. Sage Vishvamitra requests young Rama and Lakshmana to protect his sacrifices from demons Maricha and Subahu. The brothers journey through forests where Rama receives divine weapons and learns their mantras. Arriving at Mithila, Rama breaks Lord Shiva's massive bow (which no other suitor could even lift) at princess Sita's swayamvara, winning her hand. The book establishes Rama's divine lineage as Vishnu's avatar incarnated to destroy demon-king Ravana, while demonstrating precocious martial and spiritual capacities. Modern scholars including Hermann Jacobi consider this book a later interpolation absent from earliest manuscripts, evidenced by stylistic differences and narrative inconsistencies with core kandas.

- **Ayodhyakanda** (Book of Ayodhya, ~4,000 verses): King Dasharatha, aged and weary, announces plans to crown Rama as heir-apparent (yuvaraja), precipitating the epic's central crisis. Queen Kaikeyi's maidservant Manthara poisons her mind against Rama, reminding her of two boons Dasharatha granted years earlier when she saved his life in battle. Kaikeyi demands Rama's fourteen-year forest exile and her son Bharata's coronation. Despite anguished pleas, Dasharatha cannot retract his promise; Rama accepts exile without protest, demonstrating perfect filial duty. Sita insists on accompanying her husband despite Rama's arguments that forest life endangers royal women. Brother Lakshmana similarly refuses to remain, declaring devotion to Rama transcends all other bonds. The trio departs while Ayodhya mourns. Dasharatha dies from grief. Bharata, absent during these events, returns and furiously rejects the ill-gotten throne, traveling to the forest to plead with Rama to return. Rama refuses, citing dharmic duty to honor his father's promise. Bharata rules as regent, placing Rama's sandals on the throne as symbol of legitimate sovereignty, establishing model of selfless governance.

- **Aranyakanda** (Book of the Forest, ~2,900 verses): Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana wander forests, visiting ashrams of various sages who host them and describe demonic depredations. The trio settles at Panchavati on the Godavari river. Demoness Shurpanakha (sister of Ravana) encounters Rama, becomes infatuated, and proposes marriage; rebuffed, she attacks Sita. Lakshmana mutilates Shurpanakha, cutting off her nose and ears as punishment. She flees to her brother Ravana in Lanka, simultaneously seeking vengeance and inflaming his desire by describing Sita's beauty. Ravana devises scheme: his uncle Maricha transforms into golden deer; Sita desires it; Rama pursues; the deer (dying) mimics Rama's voice crying for help; Sita sends Lakshmana to assist; Ravana (disguised as ascetic) abducts the now-unguarded Sita in his aerial chariot. Vulture-king Jatayu attempts heroic rescue but Ravana mortally wounds him. This book transforms domestic exile drama into cosmic conflict between dharma and adharma, with Sita's abduction constituting the pivotal event driving all subsequent action.

- **Kishkindhakanda** (Book of Kishkindha, ~2,500 verses): Searching for Sita, Rama and Lakshmana encounter exiled monkey-prince Sugriva and his minister Hanuman near Mount Rishyamukha. Sugriva recounts his brother Vali's tyranny and unjust exile. Rama promises to kill Vali in exchange for Sugriva's assistance locating Sita. The battle ensues: Rama shoots Vali from hiding (rather than face-to-face combat), generating moral controversy explored in subsequent verses where Rama justifies his action. Dying Vali questions this apparent dharma violation; Rama argues that monkeys follow human rather than kshatriya codes, and Vali's theft of Sugriva's wife warranted punishment. Vali accepts the explanation and dies. Sugriva assumes the throne and dispatches monkey search parties in all directions to locate Sita. This book introduces Hanuman, whose absolute devotion to Rama becomes central to subsequent narrative and establishes him as paramount devotional figure in later Hindu bhakti traditions, often worshipped independently.

- **Sundarakanda** (Book of Beauty, ~2,700 verses): The southern search party led by Hanuman reaches the ocean's edge without locating Sita. Vulture Sampati (Jatayu's brother) reveals that Ravana imprisoned Sita in Lanka across the ocean. Hanuman, discovering his divine heritage as wind-god Vayu's son, leaps across the hundred-yojana strait in magnificent flight described through extended poetic passages. Entering Lanka at night, he searches the demon city and finally discovers Sita in Ashoka grove, guarded by rakshasis, refusing Ravana's advances. Hanuman delivers Rama's ring as token; Sita gives her jewel for return. Hanuman suggests carrying Sita back immediately; she refuses, insisting Rama must rescue her himself to avenge the insult. Discovered by demons, Hanuman is brought before Ravana; they set his tail aflame intending humiliation. Instead, Hanuman uses the fire to burn much of Lanka before leaping back across ocean. This book, predominantly featuring Hanuman rather than Rama, enjoys independent liturgical and devotional status, frequently recited in its entirety as act of devotion. The "beauty" reference may indicate Sita's beauty, Lanka's splendor, or the poetic excellence of the book itself.

- **Yuddhakanda** (Book of War, ~5,600 verses—longest kanda): Rama's army of monkeys and bears reaches the ocean; he performs penance to ocean-god Samudra requesting passage; when entreaty fails, threatens to dry the ocean; Samudra appears suggesting bridge construction. The army builds Rama Setu (bridge of stones) to Lanka. Ravana's brother Vibhishana defects to Rama's side, providing intelligence and counsel. Massive battle ensues with numerous single combats between champions from both sides, described through elaborate martial poetry. Lakshmana faces Ravana's son Indrajit (master of illusion) and suffers near-fatal wounding; Hanuman flies to Himalayas and brings entire mountain of healing herbs when unable to identify the specific plant needed. Various demon generals fall to Rama's arrows. The climactic battle between Rama and Ravana spans multiple days, with Ravana repeatedly reviving through his ten heads—Rama must slay all ten to ensure death. Rama finally kills Ravana using Brahmastra (supreme weapon) blessed by gods. Vibhishana becomes Lanka's king. Sita undergoes agnipariksha (trial by fire) to prove her purity maintained during captivity; Agni (fire god) himself testifies to her innocence. The triumphant return to Ayodhya in pushpaka vimana (aerial chariot) and Rama's coronation complete the book, establishing righteous governance.

- **Uttarakanda** (Book of the Aftermath, ~3,300 verses): Rama's reign establishes Ramarajya—governance characterized by prosperity, justice, dharmic order, and absence of suffering or unnatural death. However, rumors circulate questioning Sita's purity during captivity. Though Rama knows her innocence, he exiles pregnant Sita to preserve royal reputation and demonstrate that rulers must meet higher standards than subjects. Sita finds refuge in Valmiki's ashram, where she gives birth to twins Lava and Kusha. Valmiki teaches them the completed Ramayana. Years later, during Rama's ashvamedha yajna (horse sacrifice), the boys arrive and recite the epic at court. Rama recognizes his sons and summons Sita, requesting she prove her purity again through public oath. Sita calls upon Earth (her mother, as she arose from furrow) to testify; Earth splits open and reclaims Sita, who disappears into the ground. Rama, grief-stricken, eventually enters the Sarayu river and ascends to Vaikuntha (Vishnu's heaven), reuniting with Sita in divine form. This controversial book contradicts earlier books' characterization and contains elements (particularly regarding treatment of women and shudras) that led scholars to identify it as late interpolation, possibly added to address theological questions or incorporate regional legends.

### Literary Technique

The Ramayana established the shloka (anushtubh) meter as Indian epic poetry's foundational form—a thirty-two-syllable stanza constructed in four quarters of eight syllables each. This meter proved flexible enough for extemporaneous composition by oral poets while maintaining memorability and aesthetic sophistication. The shloka became the verse form par excellence for subsequent epics, Puranas, Smritis, and Sanskrit scientific treatises. Beyond metrical innovation, Valmiki employed sophisticated poetic devices: extensive metaphor and simile (warriors tall as mountains, heroes bright as sun), personification (Spring wearing flowers like garments), hyperbole amplifying heroic qualities, and systematic allusion to Vedic mythology providing narrative depth. The narrative technique employs framing devices: Valmiki's composition of the epic appears within the epic itself; Lava and Kusha's recitation at Rama's court creates meta-narrative recursion; and divine sanction (Brahma's commission) authorizes the text. The epic demonstrates psychological realism within idealized characterization—Rama embodies dharma yet suffers genuine anguish during separation from Sita; Sita represents perfect feminine virtue yet shows human vulnerability and agency; Ravana appears as villain yet receives dignified treatment with genuine majesty. The text integrates multiple rasas (aesthetic emotions): predominantly vira (heroic) and karuna (compassion), with shringara (romantic), adbhuta (wonder), raudra (fury), and shanta (peace) interwoven throughout.

### Major Episodes

Several episodes achieved particular cultural resonance beyond the complete narrative: Rama's breaking of Shiva's bow at Sita's swayamvara establishes heroic credentials through divine feat; the golden deer episode demonstrates maya (illusion) corrupting judgment; Sita's abduction by Ravana through aerial chariot represents the central crisis driving subsequent action; Jatayu's (vulture king's) fatal attempt to rescue Sita embodies heroic sacrifice; Hanuman's leap across the ocean to Lanka demonstrates devotion transcending physical limitation; the burning of Lanka after demons set Hanuman's tail aflame reverses captor-captive power dynamics; the bridge to Lanka (Rama Setu) constructed by monkey army represents collective achievement through devotion; Sita's trial by fire (agnipariksha) after rescue raises enduring questions about purity, honor, and gender; and Rama's killing of Vali from hiding generates moral ambiguity challenging simple hero-villain dichotomies. These episodes received independent artistic treatment across painting, sculpture, dance, and dramatic traditions, sometimes with interpretations challenging or reframing the textual presentation.

### Philosophical Themes

The Ramayana fundamentally explores dharma—righteous duty—through narrative rather than philosophical exposition. Rama embodies dharma personified: son who honors father's promise despite manifest injustice; warrior who fights according to kshatriya code; king who prioritizes subjects' opinion over personal desire; husband torn between love and public duty. These ideals created the Ramarajya concept—righteous governance ensuring prosperity, justice, and cosmic order—which became powerful political metaphor throughout Indian history. The epic examines relationships as loci of dharmic duty: ideal brotherhood (Rama-Lakshmana-Bharata), perfect marriage (Rama-Sita), devotional service (Hanuman-Rama), and complex antagonism (Rama-Ravana). Sita represents stri-dharma (women's duty) through absolute devotion yet demonstrates agency through her choices and ultimate return to Earth rather than remaining with Rama. The Uttarakanda's controversial portrayal of her second exile raises questions about justice, reputation, and dharma's gender dimensions that commentators debated for centuries. The epic explores karma and destiny's interplay: curses determine outcomes (Shurpanakha's mutilation triggers Sita's abduction), divine plans manifest through apparent chance (Rama as Vishnu's avatar must kill Ravana), and characters' choices within predetermined frameworks demonstrate free will's limits. The contrast between satya (truth/integrity) and politics appears in Rama's killing of Vali, which violates warrior codes yet serves larger dharmic purpose. Modern scholarship recognizes these philosophical complexities as intentional rather than contradictory, presenting dharma as contextual and requiring judgment rather than mechanical rule-following.

## Textual History

The Ramayana originated in oral tradition, with the text itself emphasizing oral transmission: Valmiki teaches the poem to disciples who "recite it throughout the land," and Lava-Kusha's performance before Rama's court dramatizes oral circulation. This oral foundation explains regional variations and the text's continuing evolution across centuries. The tradition of professional reciters (likely Brahmin specialists) maintained textual memory while introducing adaptations for local audiences. By the time of manuscript production, significant regional recensions had developed. Modern scholarship identifies two primary recensions: the Northern recension (with Northwestern and Northeastern sub-divisions) and the Southern recension, preserving substantive variations in episode details, character portrayals, and philosophical emphases. A 6th-century West Bengal manuscript notably lacks two entire kandas, providing crucial evidence for scholarly arguments about the Balakanda and Uttarakanda as interpolations. The critical edition produced by the Oriental Institute, Baroda (published 1951-1975 across seven volumes) represents monumental scholarly achievement: examining approximately 2,000 manuscripts from across India, editors worked with 50-100 manuscripts per kanda to establish a critical text approximating the archetype. This critical edition comprises roughly 18,600 verses—significantly shorter than some traditional recensions, reflecting editorial decisions to excise probable interpolations. Robert P. Goldman completed an English translation of the critical edition (finished November 2016), providing scholars worldwide access to the reconstructed text. Bibek Debroy produced another English translation of the critical edition. These scholarly reconstructions coexist with living recitation traditions preserving regional variations, devotional texts incorporating particular episodes, and performance traditions that freely adapt the narrative.

## Cultural Significance

The Ramayana profoundly shaped Hindu religious and cultural identity across South and Southeast Asia, with its characters and narratives becoming "fundamental to cultural consciousness" for billions. The text established templates for subsequent literature—narrative structures, character types, poetic conventions, and philosophical frameworks—making it architecturally foundational beyond its adi-kavya designation. The epic inspired approximately 33 traditional commentaries in Sanskrit alone, representing diverse theological and philosophical schools: Advaita Vedanta interpretations reading the epic allegorically; Vishishtadvaita commentaries emphasizing devotional relationship; and grammatical commentaries analyzing Valmiki's linguistic innovations. Beyond Sanskrit scholarship, countless regional adaptations recontextualized the narrative for particular communities and theological perspectives.

Major regional versions demonstrate the epic's adaptability while maintaining narrative recognition. Kamban's 12th-century Tamil Ramavataram (Kambaramayanam) comprises over 10,500 verses, emphasizing Tamil aesthetic principles (akam-puram poetics) and emotional depth through detailed psychological portraiture exceeding Valmiki's characterization. Krittibas Ojha's 15th-century Bengali Krittivasi Ramayan incorporated Bengali folk traditions and devotional fervor. Tulsidas's 16th-century Awadhi Ramcharitmanas fundamentally reshaped North Indian religious culture through bhakti emphasis, making Rama the supreme deity and reconfiguring the narrative as devotional scripture rather than courtly epic. This text became household scripture across Hindi-speaking regions, memorized and recited in domestic and temple settings. Ezhuthachan's 16th-century Malayalam Adhyatma Ramayanam Kilippattu ("parrot song") integrated Advaita philosophy with devotional poetry. Jain versions (Vimalasuri's 3rd-century Paumacariya in Prakrit, later Sanskrit versions) recast the narrative within Jain cosmology and ethics, making Rama and Lakshmana Jain teachers who eventually achieve moksha through Jain practice, while Ravana becomes a misguided Jain who will attain liberation in future birth. Buddhist Dasaratha Jataka presents Rama narrative as previous birth story of the Buddha. These theological reconfigurations demonstrate the narrative's malleability across religious traditions.

Performance traditions sustained the epic across centuries, making it accessible beyond literate elites while allowing improvisation and local adaptation. Ramlila dramatic enactments, especially prominent during autumn Dussehra festivals in North India, transform entire towns into temporary performance spaces where elaborate episodes unfold over nine nights, culminating in Ravana's effigy burning on Dussehra (Vijayadashami). The Ramnagar Ramlila near Varanasi, patronized by the Kashi Naresh, represents the most elaborate tradition, involving hundreds of performers and transforming the landscape into Lanka, Ayodhya, and forest settings. Shadow puppet theater in South India—particularly Karnataka's Togalu Gombeyaata and Kerala's tolpava koothu—performed all-night Ramayana episodes mixing verse recitation, prose narration, musical accompaniment, and puppet manipulation. These traditions preserve archaic performance techniques while incorporating contemporary commentary. Kathakali dance-drama in Kerala developed elaborate choreographic and facial expression (mudra) vocabularies for portraying Ramayana episodes, with performers undergoing years of rigorous training. Professional storytelling traditions (Harikatha in South India, Pravachan in North India) enabled itinerant performers to sustain themselves while disseminating the narrative across villages, adapting presentation to local dialects and concerns.

Southeast Asian transmission produced distinct national epics demonstrating remarkable cultural translation. Indonesia's 9th-10th century Kakawin Ramayana in Old Javanese adapted Sanskrit poetics to Javanese aesthetics. Continuing wayang wong (dance-drama with human performers) and wayang kulit (shadow puppet theater with leather puppets and gamelan accompaniment) traditions in Java and Bali preserve the narrative while incorporating Islamic, Hindu, and indigenous Javanese elements—Islamic Java's retention of Hindu epics through performance demonstrates cultural layering transcending religious conversion. Thailand's Ramakien, commissioned as royal court epic, incorporates Thai Buddhist cosmology, local demons and deities, and elaborate courtly aesthetics, with temple murals at Bangkok's Grand Palace depicting episodes. Cambodia's Reamker achieved classical status through royal ballet traditions, with intricate hand gestures and choreography encoding narrative details. Myanmar's Yama Zatdaw, Laos's Phra Lak Phra Lam, Malaysia's Hikayat Seri Rama, and Philippines' Maharadia Lawana adapted the narrative to Theravada Buddhism and local folklore. Archaeological evidence demonstrates transmission routes: 8th-century Prambanan temple reliefs in Java, Angkor Wat's extensive 12th-century Ramayana panels in Cambodia, Sukhothai-period Thai sculptures, all preserving iconographic conventions while developing regional artistic styles. This pan-Asian diffusion represents one of history's most successful cultural transmissions, with the epic becoming simultaneously recognizable and locally specific across vast geographical and cultural distances.

The epic's political influence appears throughout Indian history in complex ways. Pre-colonial rulers claimed descent from Rama's Solar dynasty (Suryavamsha) or modeled governance on Ramarajya ideals, using the epic to legitimize authority and articulate governance philosophy. British colonial administrators studied the text through Orientalist frameworks, simultaneously admiring its literary qualities and using it to construct essential Indian characteristics supporting colonial governance theories. Modern political movements deployed Rama narratives for identity formation with varying interpretations: Gandhi invoked Ramarajya as metaphor for ideal self-governing society based on truth and non-violence; Hindu nationalist movements literalized the narrative, treating Ayodhya as historical birthplace requiring temple reconstruction. The controversial Ayodhya site dispute—culminating in 1992 Babri Masjid demolition and 2019 Supreme Court verdict—demonstrates the narrative's continuing political potency and contested interpretation within contemporary Indian democracy.

Academic study of the Ramayana contributed across multiple disciplines. Comparative mythology scholarship, including Joseph Campbell's hero's journey framework, drew partially on Rama's quest narrative of separation, initiation, and return. Colonial-era Orientalism, beginning with translations by William Carey and others, shaped European perceptions of India as simultaneously spiritual and despotic, philosophical and caste-bound. Postcolonial literary theory examined adaptation and cultural translation processes, analyzing how regional versions negotiate between Sanskrit textual authority and local cultural autonomy. Gender studies scholars debated Sita's representation—simultaneously idealized feminine virtue and problematic model constraining women's agency—analyzing how different versions emphasize or critique her treatment. Performance studies documented living traditions and analyzed relationships between text and embodied practice. World literature frameworks examined the epic's global circulation, from 18th-century European discovery through contemporary diaspora adaptations in theater, film, television, and graphic novels. The epic's global reach demonstrates cultural products' capacity to transcend original contexts while remaining anchored in particular aesthetic and philosophical traditions, proving relevant across radically different historical moments and cultural situations.

## Rights and Public Domain Status

Valmiki's Ramayana, composed between the 7th-5th centuries BCE, exists firmly in the public domain worldwide, with no copyright restrictions on the original Sanskrit text or early translations. The Baroda Oriental Institute critical edition (1951-1975), while representing significant 20th-century editorial scholarship, presents a reconstructed ancient text based on manuscript collation rather than original creative authorship, making the edited text freely available for scholarly and public use. Early English translations from the 19th and early 20th centuries—including those by Ralph T.H. Griffith (1870-1874), Manmatha Nath Dutt (1891-1894), and Hari Prasad Shastri (1952-1959)—have entered the public domain in most jurisdictions. Robert P. Goldman's complete translation of the critical edition (finished 2016) and Bibek Debroy's translation remain under copyright protection. Regional language versions from medieval periods (Kamban's Tamil version, Krittibas's Bengali version, Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas) similarly exist in the public domain, though modern annotated editions and contemporary translations may carry copyright on editorial apparatus and translation respectively. The text's public domain status enables free scholarly research, digital dissemination, derivative adaptations, and cultural use without legal restriction, supporting the epic's continuing evolution through contemporary retellings, performance adaptations, and educational applications while preserving accessibility to foundational texts of Indian civilization.

The proliferation of digital access democratizes engagement with the text while raising questions about authoritative versions, editorial standards, and the relationship between critical editions and living traditions that continue evolving the epic through performance and devotional practice.
