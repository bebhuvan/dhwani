# The Tribes and Castes of Bengal — Anthropometric Data & Ethnographic Glossary

## Overview

Published in 1891 in four volumes, *The Tribes and Castes of Bengal* represents a landmark in colonial ethnographic practice—though one now recognized as deeply problematic in its methodology and theoretical assumptions. Herbert Hope Risley produced this work following his appointment in 1885 to oversee the Ethnographic Survey of Bengal, deploying a large staff of administrators, missionaries, and local informants to gather data over five years. The resulting publication comprised two volumes of ethnographic glossary providing alphabetically arranged entries on castes and tribes, and two volumes of anthropometric data presenting statistical measurements organized by community. According to the *Oxford Dictionary of National Biography*, the work inaugurated "a new chapter in Indian official literature," immediately reviewed across multiple countries and establishing Risley's reputation as the preeminent authority on Indian ethnography within colonial administrative circles.

The work emerged at a particular moment in colonial knowledge production when physical anthropology, racial classification schemes, and statistical enumeration converged with administrative imperatives to categorize and control subject populations. Risley's volumes provided not merely descriptive ethnography but an explicit theoretical framework arguing that caste hierarchy reflected racial stratification, with physical measurements serving as objective markers of social position. This fusion of anthropometry with caste analysis would prove extraordinarily influential in shaping census operations, administrative policy, and broader colonial understandings of Indian society, even as its scientific foundations were methodologically suspect and its political implications served to rigidify social categories that had previously been more fluid and contested.

## About the Author — H. H. Risley

Herbert Hope Risley (1851-1911) entered the Indian Civil Service in 1873 after graduating from New College, Oxford, with a second-class degree in law and modern history. Posted to Bengal, he transitioned from routine administrative duties to ethnographic research, adopting anthropometric techniques from French physical anthropologist Paul Topinard. Risley's career trajectory exemplified the colonial administrator-ethnographer, combining bureaucratic authority with claims to scientific expertise. His anthropometric survey incorporated eleven measurements including stature, biacromial breadth, nine head measurements (cephalic breadth and length, bizygomatic breadth, frontal breadth, nasal height and width, nasomalar breadth), plus somatoscopic observations of skin color, eye color, and hair texture.

Risley's administrative ascent paralleled his ethnographic prominence. Appointed Census Commissioner in 1899, he orchestrated the massive 1901 decennial census, which formally codified caste classifications throughout India. He subsequently served as Director of Ethnography (1901) and Home Secretary under Lord Curzon, receiving honors including Companion of the Indian Empire (1892), Companion of the Order of the Star of India (1904), and Knight Commander of the Order of the Indian Empire (1907). After returning to England in 1910, he became Permanent Secretary at the India Office and President of the Royal Anthropological Institute before his death in Wimbledon on September 30, 1911.

Risley's central theoretical claim—that "the social position of a caste varies inversely as its nasal index"—epitomized his conviction that physical measurements could objectively determine racial composition and thereby explain social hierarchy. By 1901, he classified Indian populations into seven racial types: Aryo-Dravidian, Dravidian, Indo-Aryan, Mongolo-Dravidian, Mongoloid, Scytho-Dravidian, and Turko-Iranian. He maintained that caste reflected "community of race" rather than occupation, asserting that racial difference constituted "the real determining principle of the caste system." This framework served not only administrative purposes but ideological ones: Risley argued that anthropometric data demonstrated India's irretrievable fragmentation by caste-as-race, proving that Indian society lacked the qualities necessary for political unity and thereby justifying continued British rule as indispensable.

## The Work

**Scope and Methodology:**

The ethnographic glossary volumes presented alphabetically arranged entries covering communities across the Bengal Presidency, incorporating information on social customs, marriage practices, occupational specializations, internal hierarchies, religious observances, and folkloric traditions. Each entry synthesized data from multiple sources including missionary accounts, administrative reports, and information from local correspondents, though Risley rarely acknowledged regional variation or disputed claims about caste ranking despite copious census evidence demonstrating such contestation. The anthropometric volumes presented statistical tables derived from measurements of selected "specimens" from various communities—though historian Crispin Bates notes that maximum sample sizes rarely exceeded 100 individuals, with many conclusions based on cranial measurements of as few as 30 people. Risley carefully selected his subjects to yield data supporting his predetermined theoretical conclusions rather than conducting representative sampling.

The work incorporated photographs and genealogical data alongside numerical measurements, presenting an appearance of comprehensive scientific documentation. However, the selection criteria for communities studied, individuals measured, and data emphasized reflected colonial administrative priorities and Risley's racial hypotheses rather than systematic ethnographic principles. The volumes distinguished between "definite" physical characteristics amenable to precise measurement (nasal index, cephalic index) and "indefinite" features (skin color, hair texture), privileging the former as objective racial markers while employing the latter for supplementary classification. This methodological framework borrowed from European physical anthropology but applied it to social categories—castes—that were neither biologically determined nor historically stable, a fundamental category error that undermined the entire enterprise.

**Theoretical Framework:**

Risley's explicit theoretical commitment to what historian Thomas Trautmann termed "the racial theory of Indian civilization" structured the entire work. He posited that Indian society resulted from Aryan conquest of indigenous Dravidian populations, with caste hierarchy reflecting degrees of racial mixture between these groups. Higher castes supposedly exhibited greater Aryan physical characteristics (narrower noses, lighter skin) while lower castes showed more pronounced Dravidian features. This framework both drew upon and reinforced British racial ideologies applied globally to justify colonial domination. The nasal index served as Risley's key diagnostic tool—he claimed that measuring the ratio of nose width to height could objectively determine racial ancestry and thereby predict social position. Contemporary anthropologist Bernard Cohn identified this racial interpretation as Risley's "theoretical axe to grind," shaping data collection and interpretation throughout.

The political implications of this framework proved consequential beyond academic anthropology. By naturalizing caste as racial and therefore biologically immutable, Risley's work suggested that social reform efforts were futile attempts to override natural hierarchies. His theory supported administrative policies treating castes as discrete, bounded communities with fixed characteristics and rankings—a view that census operations, guided by Risley's principles, institutionalized through official enumeration. Census superintendents were instructed to establish definitive caste rankings in their provinces, though they reported that the task proved difficult and consultative committees frequently disagreed. Risley never acknowledged such evidence contradicting his theoretical assumptions about objective, measurable social precedence.

## Significance

**Contemporary Reception:**

Within colonial administrative and scholarly circles, the work achieved immediate authoritative status. *The Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society* reviewed it extensively, and census operations increasingly incorporated Risley's classificatory schemes and theoretical premises. His appointment as Census Commissioner in 1899 institutionalized his approach, making the 1901 census the vehicle through which his racial-caste theory was systematically applied across India. Census administrators created rigid caste categories and attempted to establish definitive rankings where previously such boundaries had been negotiable and hierarchies contested. The work provided colonial officials with apparently scientific justification for viewing Indian society as irremediably divided and therefore incapable of self-governance—a conclusion Risley explicitly drew from his anthropometric data.

**Later Assessment:**

Modern scholarship has subjected Risley's work to devastating critique on multiple grounds. Methodologically, the sample sizes were grossly inadequate for the sweeping generalizations Risley advanced, with selection bias ensuring results confirmed predetermined theories. The fundamental premise that caste constituted a racial rather than social category has been thoroughly discredited—genetic studies demonstrate no systematic correlation between caste position and biological ancestry of the sort Risley's theory required. Historian Crispin Bates characterized Risley's career as "the apotheosis of pseudo-scientific racism." Scholars including Nicholas Dirks, Bernard Cohn, and Susan Bayly have analyzed how colonial ethnography, exemplified by Risley's work, actively constructed and rigidified caste categories that had previously been more fluid, situational, and contested. Far from objectively documenting pre-existing social structures, Risley's taxonomies and the census operations they informed created new social realities through the power of colonial classification.

The work contributed significantly to what scholars term "caste rigidification"—the process whereby colonial knowledge production, administrative practice, and legal frameworks transformed caste from a complex, regionally variable, and negotiable aspect of social organization into fixed, enumerated, legally consequential identity categories. Census classification required individuals and communities to claim singular caste identities and accept official rankings, with economic and political implications including resource distribution, political representation, and legal status. These colonial classifications persisted into independent India's reservation system and contemporary caste politics, making Risley's work not merely a historical curiosity but a document whose classificatory violence continues to shape social realities.

**Value for Researchers:**

Despite its profound methodological and theoretical flaws, the work retains historical value for scholars studying colonial knowledge production, the history of anthropology, and caste formation. It exemplifies how nineteenth-century racial science was deployed to naturalize social hierarchies and legitimate colonial rule. The ethnographic descriptions, though filtered through colonial perspectives and informant biases, provide historical data on specific communities, occupations, and practices in late nineteenth-century Bengal, useful when approached critically with awareness of the text's limitations and ideological commitments. The work documents colonial administrative categories and practices that structured governance and continue to influence contemporary Indian society. For historians of science, it illustrates the intersection of anthropometry, statistics, and racial ideology in imperial contexts. Scholars examining caste debates necessarily engage with Risley's work as a influential—if fundamentally flawed—intervention that shaped subsequent discourse and policy.
