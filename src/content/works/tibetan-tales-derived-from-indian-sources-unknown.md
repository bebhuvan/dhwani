  This collection presents Tibetan folktales and narrative traditions that originated in Indian sources, documenting the complex transmission of stories, philosophical concepts, and literary forms from India to Tibet through centuries of Buddhist cultural exchange. Following Buddhism's establishment in Tibet (beginning 7th-8th centuries), extensive translation projects rendered Indian Buddhist texts, including jataka tales (stories of Buddha's previous births), avadana (edifying narratives), and other story literature into Tibetan, while these narratives simultaneously entered Tibetan oral tradition and were adapted to local contexts. The tales exemplify processes of cross-cultural literary transmission: Indian stories traveled to Tibet through Buddhist missionary activity, translations of Sanskrit and Pali texts into Tibetan, and oral networks of teachers and pilgrims, undergoing transformation as they were recontextualized within Tibetan cultural frameworks, merged with indigenous narrative traditions, and adapted to express Tibetan Buddhist concerns. Many stories demonstrate clear continuities with Indian sources—shared plot structures, character types, moral lessons, and philosophical themes—while showing distinctively Tibetan elaborations, modifications, and interpretations. The collection documents Buddhism's role as vector for literary and cultural transmission across Asian civilizations, revealing how religious conversion and text translation enabled circulation of narrative traditions that transcended their original contexts while remaining recognizably connected to source materials. Tales derived from Indian sources often served pedagogical functions in Tibetan Buddhist contexts: illustrating karmic principles, exemplifying virtues like generosity and compassion, providing narrative frameworks for philosophical teachings, and entertaining while instructing. Scholarly study of such transmission processes illuminates mechanisms of cross-cultural literary influence: how stories travel, what elements remain stable across cultures, what gets transformed in translation and adaptation, and how narrative traditions both preserve and innovate as they circulate through diverse cultural contexts. The work contributes to understanding the vast cultural sphere of Buddhist Asia, where shared religious tradition facilitated exchange of texts, ideas, artistic forms, and stories across linguistic and political boundaries, creating pan-Asian Buddhist literary culture while generating distinctive regional traditions.
