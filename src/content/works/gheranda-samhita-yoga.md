The Gheranda Samhita, composed in the late 17th century CE, stands as one of the three foundational texts of Hatha Yoga alongside the Hatha Yoga Pradipika (15th century) and the Shiva Samhita (17th-18th century). The text comprises 351 verses organized into seven chapters, structured as a pedagogical dialogue between sage Gheranda and his disciple Chanda Kapali. Fourteen manuscripts have been discovered across regions from Bengal to Rajasthan, indicating the text's probable northeast Indian origin.

The work presents a distinctive seven-limbed yoga system (saptanga yoga) that diverges from Patanjali's classical eight-limbed path (ashtanga yoga), the six-limbed system of the Goraksha Samhita, and the four-limbed approach in the Hatha Yoga Pradipika. Gheranda terms this system Ghatastha Yoga, meaning "vessel yoga," conceptualizing the body and mind as vessels that carry and serve the soul. The seven limbs follow a systematic progression: shatkarma (purification), asana (strengthening), mudra (steadying), pratyahara (sense-withdrawal), pranayama (breath control), dhyana (meditation), and samadhi (absorption).

The first chapter addresses shatkarma, six categories of cleansing actions encompassing more than twenty specific practices. Gheranda prescribes these purification techniques as the mandatory first stage before any other yogic practice. The six primary categories follow a specific order: dhauti (internal cleansing), basti (colon cleansing), neti (nasal cleansing), nauli (abdominal churning), trataka (concentrated gazing), and kapalbhati (frontal brain cleansing). These techniques align with Ayurvedic principles and establish the foundation for subsequent practices.

The second chapter details thirty-two asanas for building bodily strength, though the text acknowledges Shiva's teaching of 8,400,000 asanas, of which eighty-four are considered preeminent. Gheranda provides precise technical instructions for each posture, representing the most comprehensive classical treatment of asanas among Hatha Yoga texts. The Hatha Yoga Pradipika, by comparison, describes only fifteen asanas. The Gheranda Samhita's asana descriptions are predominantly sitting and contemplative, with vrksasana (tree pose) being the only standing posture detailed.

The third chapter presents twenty-five mudras (seals or gestures) along with bandhas (locks) and their execution techniques. These practices aim to perfect bodily steadiness and channel energy flows through the subtle body. Mudras serve as bridges between physical and energetic dimensions, stabilizing the practitioner for deeper internal work.

The fourth chapter addresses pratyahara through five distinct methods for sense-withdrawal. This limb transitions practitioners from external orientation to internal awareness, preparing the mind for concentrated meditation practices.

The fifth chapter systematically presents pranayama through ten specific breathing techniques. Uniquely, Gheranda integrates mantra recitation with pranayama practices, adding vibrational dimensions that affect the chakras and facilitate greater control over prana (vital energy). This integration distinguishes the text's approach from other classical sources.

The sixth chapter explicates dhyana (meditation) through three progressive stages, systematically developing internal perception and concentration. These stages build upon the foundation established by the preceding limbs, demonstrating the text's methodical pedagogical structure.

The seventh and final chapter addresses samadhi, presenting six types of absorptive states that constitute the ultimate goal of yogic practice. These states represent complete liberation and bliss through dissolution of subject-object duality.

The Gheranda Samhita relates to its companion texts through both continuity and innovation. All three classical Hatha Yoga texts share foundational practices, with similarities particularly evident in the most important asanas, which bear identical names across texts. However, the Gheranda Samhita distinguishes itself as the most encyclopedic, providing comprehensive detail unmatched by the Hatha Yoga Pradipika or Shiva Samhita. Its unique seven-limbed structure does not appear in other Hatha Yoga sources, while its emphasis on shatkarma as the necessary first stage differs from the Hatha Yoga Pradipika's sequence.

The text's influence on modern yoga practice has been profound and multifaceted. Its detailed asana descriptions provided technical specifications that informed contemporary postural practice. The comprehensive presentation of purification techniques contributed to therapeutic and holistic health programs, with shatkarma practices finding validation in wellness contexts. The systematic progression from physical purification through breath control to meditative absorption established a model that modern yoga pedagogy continues to follow. The text's accessibility as a step-by-step teaching manual facilitated transmission across generations and cultural boundaries.

Scholar Ashwini Aggarwal has characterized the Gheranda Samhita as "The Foundation of Modern Yoga," reflecting its extensive impact on contemporary practice. The text demonstrates Hatha Yoga's sophisticated integration of physical preparation and spiritual development, rejecting any separation between bodily discipline and transcendent realization. By presenting the body not as an obstacle but as the necessary vessel for spiritual attainment, the Gheranda Samhita articulates a distinctly embodied approach to liberation that distinguishes Hatha Yoga from purely contemplative traditions. The work remains a primary reference for practitioners and scholars investigating classical yoga methodology, offering detailed technical knowledge combined with a comprehensive philosophical framework.
