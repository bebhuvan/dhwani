# Catalogue of Sanskrit and Pali Books in the British Museum

## Overview

The "Catalogue of Sanskrit and Pali Books in the British Museum" constitutes one of the nineteenth century's most significant bibliographical projects documenting South Asian textual heritage in European institutional collections. First published in London by Trübner in 1876, this comprehensive reference work systematically catalogued the British Museum's holdings of printed books in Sanskrit, Pali, and later Prakrit, creating essential scholarly infrastructure for accessing one of Europe's premier collections of South Asian literature. The catalogue emerged from the British Museum's Department of Oriental Printed Books and Manuscripts, established as a unified administrative unit in 1891 (having operated as separate departments since 1867), reflecting Victorian Britain's institutional commitment to organizing and making accessible its rapidly growing collections of non-Western materials.

The catalogue's publication coincided with a crucial period in the development of Indological and Buddhist studies in Europe. By the 1870s, European universities had established professorships in Sanskrit, Oriental scholars had begun systematic study of Buddhist texts in Pali and Sanskrit, and colonial administrators and missionaries had collected substantial quantities of South Asian manuscripts and printed materials. However, these dispersed holdings remained difficult for scholars to locate and access. The British Museum's catalogue addressed this need by providing detailed bibliographic information—titles, authors, publication details, physical descriptions—enabling researchers to identify relevant texts and request access to the museum's collections.

The work represents a collaborative achievement spanning multiple generations of British orientalists. The initial 1876 volume documented the museum's foundational Sanskrit and Pali holdings. An expanded volume covering acquisitions from 1876 to 1892, compiled by Cecil Bendall (1856-1906), appeared in 1893, incorporating the significant materials acquired during a period of intensive collecting activity. A supplementary catalogue covering 1892-1906, compiled by Lionel D. Barnett (1871-1960), extended documentation through the early twentieth century. Additionally, specialized catalogues addressed the museum's manuscript collections, including Bendall's "Catalogue of the Sanskrit Manuscripts in the British Museum" (1902).

These successive volumes chart not merely the growth of the museum's collections but the evolution of British engagement with South Asian textual traditions. The catalogue documented materials ranging from classical Sanskrit literature (poetry, drama, epics) to Buddhist Pali canonical texts, from philosophical treatises of diverse schools (Vedanta, Nyaya, Sankhya, Buddhist) to grammatical and lexicographical works, from Jain scriptures in Prakrit to scientific texts on astronomy, medicine, and mathematics. This comprehensive scope reflected both the breadth of South Asian literary production and British scholars' expanding interests across diverse domains of Indian intellectual history.

The catalogue also represents a crucial moment in the institutional history of Oriental studies. The establishment of the Department of Oriental Printed Books and Manuscripts as a distinct administrative unit, separate from the general Manuscripts and Printed Books departments, acknowledged the specialized expertise required for cataloguing non-Western materials and the growing significance of Oriental collections within European research institutions. The scholars who compiled these catalogues—Haas, Bendall, Barnett—were not merely librarians but accomplished orientalists who contributed substantially to Sanskrit and Pali scholarship through their own research, translations, and critical editions.

## The British Museum's Department of Oriental Printed Books and Manuscripts

The institutional context for this catalogue's creation merits detailed examination, as it illuminates the organizational infrastructure supporting Victorian Oriental scholarship. The British Museum, founded in 1753 and opened to the public in 1759, initially focused on natural history, antiquities, and manuscripts from the founding collections (Old Royal, Sloane, Harleian, and Cottonian). Oriental materials remained comparatively few until the nineteenth century's expansion of British imperial and commercial engagement with Asia.

Until the mid-nineteenth century, Oriental books and manuscripts were administered within the general Manuscripts and Printed Books departments without specialized organizational structures. However, several factors converged to necessitate separate administrative arrangements. First, the quantity of Oriental acquisitions increased dramatically through purchase, donation, and systematic collecting efforts in British India and other Asian territories. Second, the materials' linguistic and bibliographic complexity required specialized expertise unavailable among generalist curators. Third, growing scholarly interest in Oriental studies created demand for better organized and more accessible collections.

In 1867, recognizing these needs, the Museum's Trustees established a separate Department of Oriental Manuscripts under its own Keeper. This department administered manuscript collections in Arabic, Persian, Turkish, Hebrew, Chinese, Japanese, Sanskrit, and other Asian languages. However, printed Oriental books remained within the general Printed Books department, creating administrative inefficiencies when scholars needed to consult both manuscripts and printed materials from the same textual tradition.

The solution came in 1891 with the creation of a unified Department of Oriental Printed Books and Manuscripts, bringing all Oriental materials under single administrative supervision. Sir Robert Kennaway Douglas (1838-1913), a distinguished Chinese scholar who had served as Professor of Chinese at King's College London, became the department's first Keeper. This consolidation enabled more coherent collection development, more efficient cataloguing, and better service to researchers working across manuscript and print formats.

The department's mandate encompassed materials in dozens of languages from across Asia and North Africa: Sanskrit, Pali, Prakrit, Hindi, Bengali, Tamil, Telugu, and other South Asian languages; Arabic, Persian, Turkish, and other Islamic literary languages; Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and other East Asian languages; Hebrew, Syriac, Ethiopic, and other Semitic languages; Armenian, Georgian, and other Caucasian languages; Tibetan, Mongolian, and other Central Asian languages. This extraordinary linguistic range required curatorial staff with specialized philological training and cultural knowledge.

The department attracted some of Britain's most distinguished orientalists. Beyond Douglas, its staff and associates included Cecil Bendall (Sanskrit), Lionel David Barnett (Sanskrit and Jain studies), Robert Neil Cust (Indian languages), Charles Rieu (Persian), George Margoliouth (Arabic and Hebrew), and others who combined curatorial responsibilities with active scholarship. Their dual roles as library administrators and research scholars shaped the catalogues they produced, which served not merely as finding aids but as scholarly contributions documenting textual traditions and establishing bibliographic standards.

The department produced numerous specialized catalogues beyond the Sanskrit and Pali series: catalogues of Arabic, Persian, Turkish, Hebrew, Chinese, Japanese, and Tibetan materials, among others. These publications established the British Museum as a premier center for Oriental studies research, rivaling university libraries in Oxford, Cambridge, and Paris. Scholars worldwide consulted these catalogues when planning research, identifying sources, and verifying textual information.

When the British Library separated from the British Museum in 1973, becoming an independent institution, these Oriental collections transferred to the new library, forming the core of what is now the British Library's Asian and African Collections. This department continues the cataloguing and research traditions established in the nineteenth century, though with modern technological infrastructure and evolving scholarly priorities.

## Cecil Bendall and the Catalogue's Principal Compilers

The catalogue's creation depended on the specialized expertise and scholarly dedication of several distinguished orientalists whose biographies illuminate Victorian pathways into Sanskrit and Pali scholarship.

**Cecil Bendall (1856-1906)** stands as the catalogue's most significant contributor, compiling the major expansion covering 1876-1892 acquisitions and producing the separate manuscript catalogue. Born in Islington, London, on July 1, 1856, Bendall attended the City of London School from 1869 to 1875, where George Frederick Nicholl introduced him to Sanskrit beginning in 1873. This early exposure proved decisive, shaping his career trajectory toward Oriental scholarship. He continued Sanskrit studies at Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge, earning election to a fellowship.

From 1882 to 1893, Bendall served as senior assistant in the British Museum's Department of Oriental Manuscripts and Printed Books, the period during which he compiled the catalogue's major expansion. His museum work combined administrative cataloguing responsibilities with original research on Sanskrit texts. Crucially, in 1894-1895, the British Museum sponsored Bendall's collecting expedition to Nepal and Northern India, where he acquired numerous rare Sanskrit manuscripts, significantly enriching the museum's holdings. This fieldwork provided him direct experience with South Asian manuscript traditions, textual transmission practices, and contemporary pandits' scholarly communities.

Bendall's academic career progressed alongside his museum work. He served as Professor of Sanskrit at University College London from 1895 to 1902, then succeeded his former teacher Professor E. B. Cowell as Cambridge's Professor of Sanskrit in 1903, holding this prestigious chair until his death in 1906. His scholarly contributions extended beyond cataloguing to editing and translating important Sanskrit texts, including Buddhist philosophical works. His premature death at age 50 cut short a remarkably productive career that had significantly advanced both institutional organization of Sanskrit materials and scholarly understanding of specific textual traditions.

**Ernst Anton Max Haas (1835-1882)**, associated with the catalogue's earlier stages, contributed to establishing its bibliographic frameworks and organizational principles. Though less extensively documented than Bendall, Haas represented the generation of mid-Victorian orientalists who built institutional foundations for the later flourishing of Indological studies.

**Lionel David Barnett (1871-1960)** compiled the supplementary catalogue covering 1892-1906 acquisitions, extending documentation into the twentieth century. Born a generation after Bendall, Barnett pursued an exceptionally long and productive scholarly career. He joined the British Museum staff in 1899, eventually becoming Keeper of Oriental Printed Books and Manuscripts from 1908 to 1936. His scholarly interests encompassed Sanskrit literature, Jainism, and South Indian epigraphy. Unlike Bendall, whose career centered on university teaching after leaving the museum, Barnett spent his entire professional life in the institution, shaping its collections and cataloguing practices for nearly four decades. His numerous publications included translations of Sanskrit classics, studies of Jain literature, and contributions to South Indian historical scholarship. He lived to see the British Museum's Oriental collections become foundational resources for a vastly expanded international community of scholars studying South Asian civilization.

These scholars embodied the Victorian model of the scholar-administrator: combining institutional responsibilities with active research, producing both service infrastructure (catalogues, finding aids) and original scholarship (critical editions, translations, studies). Their dual roles meant the catalogues they produced reflected not merely bureaucratic listing but informed scholarly judgment about textual significance, proper classification, and bibliographic information most useful to research.

## The Catalogue's Scope, Structure, and Contents

Understanding what the catalogue documented and how it organized information illuminates its scholarly utility and the textual landscape of the British Museum's South Asian collections.

### Scope and Coverage

The catalogue documented *printed books* in Sanskrit, Pali, and Prakrit—distinguished from the separate manuscript catalogues that addressed handwritten materials. This focus on printed texts reflected the nineteenth century's transformation of South Asian textual circulation. Traditional Sanskrit and Pali learning had depended on manuscript transmission, with texts copied by scribes and circulated through guru-student relationships and monastic libraries. The introduction of print technology in South Asia, beginning in the late eighteenth century and expanding dramatically in the nineteenth, revolutionized textual accessibility.

British missionaries, Oriental scholars, and Indian reformers established presses publishing Sanskrit texts in Devanagari and other scripts, Pali texts for Buddhist studies, and Prakrit texts for Jain scholarship. Calcutta, Bombay, Poona, Benares, Madras, and Colombo became centers of Sanskrit and Pali printing. European presses in London, Paris, and German cities also published scholarly editions of South Asian texts. The British Museum collected broadly across these publishing centers, acquiring both Indian-printed editions and European scholarly editions.

The catalogue's contents spanned the extraordinary diversity of South Asian literary and intellectual production:

**Classical Sanskrit Literature**: The museum held major works of kāvya (ornate poetry) including Kalidasa's plays and poems, the epics Ramayana and Mahabharata in various editions, Puranas (religious and mythological compendia), and narrative literature.

**Philosophical and Religious Texts**: Holdings encompassed the foundational texts of diverse darshanas (philosophical schools)—Vedanta sutras and commentaries, Sankhya texts, Nyaya logical treatises, Yoga literature—as well as Buddhist philosophical works in Sanskrit presenting Madhyamaka, Yogacara, and other schools. Jain philosophical texts in Sanskrit and Prakrit complemented Hindu and Buddhist materials.

**Buddhist Pali Canon**: The museum's Pali holdings included portions of the Tipitaka (the three-basket Pali canon), Pali commentaries, chronicles like the Mahavamsa documenting Sri Lankan Buddhist history, and post-canonical Buddhist literature. These materials supported the growing European scholarly interest in early Buddhism and Theravada traditions.

**Grammatical and Linguistic Works**: The catalogue documented grammars (including the foundational Ashtadhyayi of Panini and later grammatical texts), lexicons, and works on Sanskrit poetics and rhetoric. These materials were essential for European scholars learning Sanskrit and for understanding Indian grammatical traditions.

**Scientific Literature**: Holdings included jyotisha (astronomy and astrology) texts, ayurveda (medical) treatises, texts on mathematics and calculation, and works on architecture and other technical subjects, reflecting the breadth of Sanskrit scientific literature.

**Legal and Moral Literature**: Dharmashastra texts on Hindu law, arthashastra texts on statecraft and political economy (including editions of the Arthashastra), and nibandhas (legal digests) documented Indian jurisprudential and ethical traditions.

This comprehensive scope meant the catalogue served scholars across diverse specializations: literary historians studying classical Sanskrit poetry, philosophers examining Vedanta or Buddhist thought, historians of science investigating Indian astronomy, legal historians studying dharmashastra, and religious studies scholars exploring Hindu, Buddhist, or Jain traditions.

### Organizational Structure

The catalogue employed alphabetical organization by title within language sections (Sanskrit, Pali, Prakrit), following conventions standard in nineteenth-century library cataloguing. Each entry provided:

- **Title**: In romanized transliteration, with variant titles noted
- **Author**: Where known, with biographical dates if available
- **Place of Publication**: Indicating geographic distribution of printing centers
- **Publisher**: Documenting the presses and publishing houses involved in South Asian textual production
- **Date**: Essential for tracking edition history and publication chronology
- **Physical Description**: Pages, format, illustrations, noting material characteristics
- **Call Number**: For locating items within the museum's storage systems

This standardized structure enabled users to quickly identify relevant materials, compare editions, and request items for consultation. The romanization systems employed reflected nineteenth-century conventions, which evolved over the catalogue's publication span as scholarly standards developed.

Supplementary catalogues employed similar structures while documenting newly acquired materials, creating cumulative documentation that researchers could consult across multiple volumes to achieve comprehensive coverage of the museum's holdings.

## Historical Significance and Scholarly Impact

The catalogue's importance extended beyond its immediate function as a finding aid to encompass broader impacts on scholarship, institutional practices, and knowledge politics.

### Enabling Research Infrastructure

Most fundamentally, the catalogue made the British Museum's Sanskrit and Pali collections discoverable and accessible to scholars worldwide. Before its publication, researchers had limited means to determine what materials the museum held, requiring correspondence with staff or in-person visits to browse collections. The catalogue enabled scholars in Cambridge, Oxford, Edinburgh, European universities, and increasingly in Indian universities to identify relevant texts, verify bibliographic information, and plan research requiring specific sources.

This infrastructure particularly benefited emerging fields like Buddhist studies, where access to Pali canonical texts and Sanskrit Buddhist philosophical works proved essential. Scholars could consult the catalogue to determine which portions of the Pali canon the museum held, identify commentarial literature, and locate texts unavailable in their own institutional libraries.

### Establishing Cataloguing Standards

The catalogue helped establish bibliographic standards for documenting South Asian materials in European libraries. Questions of romanization, how to handle texts without clear authorship, how to classify materials crossing Western generic boundaries, and how to document manuscript versus printed editions required systematic solutions. The British Museum's cataloguing practices, embodied in published catalogues, influenced other institutions developing their own Oriental collections, creating increasing standardization that facilitated scholarly communication and resource sharing.

### Documenting Colonial Knowledge Collection

From contemporary critical perspectives, the catalogue also documents the politics of colonial knowledge accumulation. The British Museum's Sanskrit and Pali collections grew substantially through purchase of materials in India, acquisition of collections assembled by British colonial administrators and missionaries, and systematic collecting efforts like Bendall's Nepal expedition. This accumulation removed significant quantities of South Asian textual heritage from its regions of origin, concentrating it in London where it served primarily European scholarly and imperial purposes.

The catalogue's existence made this accumulated heritage accessible primarily to European scholars with the resources to visit London or the institutional connections to request materials. While some Indian scholars could access these collections, the asymmetries of colonial power meant European institutions like the British Museum shaped whose scholarship and what interpretive frameworks dominated emerging Indological disciplines.

### Transitions to Digital Access

In contemporary contexts, the British Museum/British Library's Sanskrit and Pali collections remain significant research resources, now accessible through digital catalogues and, increasingly, through digitization projects making actual texts available online. The nineteenth-century printed catalogues have themselves been digitized, available through platforms like Internet Archive and HathiTrust, enabling scholars to consult historical bibliography while also accessing current integrated library systems.

This transition to digital infrastructure transforms the catalogues' significance. Initially created to make physical collections discoverable to scholars who might travel to London, the digitized catalogues now serve primarily as historical documents illuminating nineteenth-century collecting practices, cataloguing methodologies, and the institutional history of Oriental studies. Modern researchers searching for Sanskrit or Pali texts typically consult digital databases and online catalogues offering more comprehensive coverage and sophisticated search capabilities than the nineteenth-century printed volumes provided.

However, these historical catalogues retain scholarly value for understanding which texts circulated in nineteenth-century print editions, documenting the publication history of important works, and reconstructing the scholarly infrastructure supporting Victorian Indology and Buddhist studies. They represent artifacts of an era when European institutions dominated the study of Asian textual traditions, establishing interpretive frameworks and scholarly methodologies that shaped these fields for generations.

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

More than a century after the initial catalogue's publication, its significance persists across multiple dimensions. For historians of scholarship, it documents the institutional and bibliographic infrastructure enabling Victorian Oriental studies. For bibliographers, it records nineteenth-century Sanskrit and Pali printing and publication patterns. For scholars of colonialism, it materializes the knowledge-gathering dimensions of British imperialism. For contemporary researchers, it occasionally provides unique documentation of texts no longer readily available or of edition information supplementing modern bibliographic databases.

The tradition it established—systematic cataloguing of South Asian materials in European institutions—continues in contemporary forms through the British Library's ongoing cataloguing work, international cataloguing projects like the Catalogus Catalogorum documenting Sanskrit literature, and collaborative digital initiatives creating shared bibliographic infrastructure accessible globally.

The cataloguers themselves—Bendall, Barnett, Haas—represent a scholarly generation that established academic Indology and Buddhist studies as recognized disciplines with institutional homes in universities and museums. Their dual roles as administrators and scholars shaped fields that subsequently evolved in diverse directions: from the Orientalism they practiced toward more critical, postcolonial approaches; from European dominance toward global participation including substantial scholarship by South Asian and Asian scholars; from print-based methods toward digital humanities and computational approaches.

The "Catalogue of Sanskrit and Pali Books in the British Museum" thus stands as a monument to Victorian scholarship's accomplishments and limitations: its impressive systematic achievements in documenting textual traditions and its entanglement with imperial power structures that shaped whose knowledge, what texts, and which interpretive traditions gained authority and circulation.
