# A History of Indian Philosophy

## Overview

*A History of Indian Philosophy* represents the most comprehensive English-language survey of Indian philosophical traditions produced in the first half of the twentieth century. Published by Cambridge University Press in five volumes between 1922 and 1955, this monumental work traces the development of Indian thought from the Vedic Samhitas through medieval bhakti movements and southern Shaiva schools. Volume I (1922) established Dasgupta's methodology and covered Buddhist and Jaina philosophy alongside the six orthodox (astika) Hindu systems. Volume II (1932) completed the treatment of Samkhya, Yoga, Purva Mimamsa, and Nyaya-Vaisheshika, with extensive analysis of the Advaita Vedanta of Shankara. Volume III (1940) examined dualistic and qualified non-dualistic Vedanta traditions, including Bhaskara, Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita, Nimbarka, and Vijnanabhiksu's Samkhya-Yoga synthesis. Volume IV (1949) addressed the Bhagavata Purana, Madhva's Dvaita school, Vallabha's Shuddhadvaita, and the Gaudiya Vaishnava philosophy of Chaitanya, Jiva Gosvami, and Baladeva Vidyabhushana. Volume V (1955), completed posthumously with assistance from Dasgupta's widow Surama, covered southern Shaiva schools including Shaiva Siddhanta and Vira Shaivism.

Dasgupta's work distinguished itself from earlier surveys through systematic engagement with original Sanskrit, Pali, and Prakrit sources, including unpublished manuscripts. Rather than presenting philosophical systems as isolated doctrines or purely historical artifacts, he analyzed their internal logical structures and epistemological foundations. His treatment emphasized both fundamental points of agreement across traditions and distinctive positions on metaphysics, epistemology, and soteriology. The comprehensive scope extended beyond the six classical darshanas to encompass heterodox schools, sectarian philosophical developments, and regional traditions often marginalized in earlier scholarship.

## About the Author — Surendranath Dasgupta

Surendranath Dasgupta (1887-1952) was born in Kushtia, Bengal (now Bangladesh) and received rigorous training in both traditional Sanskrit scholarship and modern Western philosophy. After graduating with honors in Sanskrit from Ripon College, Calcutta, he earned master's degrees in Sanskrit (1908) from Sanskrit College, Calcutta, and Western philosophy (1910) from the University of Calcutta. His doctoral research at Cambridge University under the supervision of philosopher J.M.E. McTaggart culminated in his PhD (1920) and the publication of the first volume of the *History* in 1921. This dual formation in Indian textual traditions and European philosophical methodology shaped his analytical approach throughout the five-volume work. McTaggart's idealist philosophy and emphasis on systematic conceptual analysis influenced Dasgupta's treatment of Indian metaphysical systems, though he avoided imposing Western categories inappropriately on Indian materials.

Dasgupta held academic positions at Rajshahi College, Chittagong College (as Professor of Sanskrit and Bengali), and lectured at Cambridge before joining Presidency College, Kolkata as Professor of Philosophy in 1924. He later served as Principal of Sanskrit College and occupied the King George V Chair of Mental and Moral Science at the University of Calcutta (1942-1945). During 1945-1950, while in England, he completed Volume IV and engaged with British philosophical circles while maintaining connections to Indian academic institutions. He subsequently held a readership in Philosophy at Lucknow University, where he died in December 1952 shortly after finishing the manuscript of Volume V. His other major works include *Hindu Mysticism* (1927), which examined yogic and tantric practices, *Yoga Philosophy in Relation to Other Systems of Indian Thought* (1930), and studies of Rabindranath Tagore's philosophical poetry. Dasgupta received the Griffith Prize (1952) for his contributions to Sanskrit scholarship and fellowships from the Indian Philosophical Congress (serving as president in 1932), the Royal Society of Literature, and the Academy of Sciences of Poland. His scholarly networks extended across European and Indian institutions, facilitating access to manuscript collections and scholarly exchanges essential for the *History*'s comprehensive scope.

## The Work

**Scope and Methodology:**

Volume I established the analytical framework through examination of Vedic philosophy in the Samhitas, Brahmanas, and Aranyakas, followed by Upanishadic doctrines of Brahman and Atman from the seventh to sixth centuries BCE. Dasgupta engaged with philological debates over Vedic chronology while focusing on philosophical concepts rather than ritual technicalities. The treatment of Buddhist philosophy encompassed Theravada canonical texts, Abhidhamma scholasticism, Madhyamika dialectic of Nagarjuna, and Yogachara/Vijnanavada epistemology developed by Dignaga and Dharmakirti. Each Buddhist school received analysis of its distinctive positions on causation, consciousness, and the nature of reality. Jaina philosophy received systematic analysis of its distinctive anekantavada (non-absolutism) and syadvada (conditional predication), emphasizing epistemological relativity and the seven-fold predication (saptabhangi). The orthodox systems—Samkhya metaphysical dualism distinguishing purusha and prakriti, Yoga meditation practice and philosophy as systematized in Patanjali's Yoga-sutras, Nyaya logic and epistemology with its theory of valid cognition (pramana), Vaisheshika atomism and categories (padartha), Purva Mimamsa hermeneutics and ritual philosophy—each received chapters analyzing foundational texts and commentarial traditions spanning multiple centuries. Volume I concluded with Shankara's Advaita Vedanta, examining the Brahma-sutra-bhashya and commentaries on the Upanishads, with particular attention to the doctrine of maya and the distinction between vyavaharika (empirical) and paramarthika (absolute) reality.

Volume II continued the Advaita tradition through post-Shankara developments, including Padmapada, Sureshvara, Vachaspati Mishra, and Vidyaranya. Volume III shifted to pluralistic and dualistic schools: Bhaskara's modification of Advaita, the Pancharatra Agamas, the Tamil Alvars' devotional poetry, Yamunacharya's epistemology, Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita with its doctrine of qualified non-dualism, Nimbarka's Dvaitadvaita, and Vijnanabhiksu's sixteenth-century synthesis of Samkhya and Vedanta. Philosophical speculation in selected Puranas received detailed treatment. Volume IV examined the Bhagavata Purana's philosophical dimensions, Madhva's strict dualism (Dvaita) distinguishing five fundamental differences, Vallabha's Shuddhadvaita emphasizing Krishna bhakti, and Gaudiya Vaishnavism's development in Bengal through Chaitanya's theology and Jiva Gosvami's systematic elaboration in the Sat-sandarbha. The volume devoted substantial space to debates between Advaitins and Dvaitins on the nature of reality, causation, and the relationship between Brahman and individual souls.

Volume V addressed southern Shaiva traditions largely ignored in earlier surveys: Shaiva Siddhanta's dualistic theism, Basava's Vira Shaivism (Lingayat philosophy), Shrikantha's Shaiva Vedanta interpreting the Brahma-sutras from a Shaiva perspective, and Shaiva philosophical developments in Puranic literature. This final volume demonstrated Dasgupta's commitment to comprehensive coverage beyond northern Brahmanical traditions.

**Historical Context:**

The *History* emerged from the early twentieth-century movement to establish Indian philosophy as a legitimate field of academic study in Western universities. Cambridge University Press's publication signaled institutional recognition of Indian thought as worthy of serious philosophical engagement rather than mere antiquarian or religious interest. Dasgupta's training under McTaggart, a prominent British idealist philosopher, influenced his systematic approach to analyzing conceptual structures and logical arguments within each tradition. McTaggart's emphasis on rigorous metaphysical analysis and the examination of philosophical systems as coherent wholes shaped Dasgupta's methodology, even as he resisted crude analogies between Indian and Western traditions. The work responded to Max Müller's *Six Systems of Indian Philosophy* (1899), which had provided an accessible but now dated introduction to the orthodox darshanas. Müller's work, while pioneering, relied heavily on secondary sources and presented simplified accounts of complex philosophical debates. Dasgupta's far more extensive treatment, grounded in original sources including unpublished manuscripts and extensive commentarial literature, aimed to demonstrate the philosophical sophistication and historical complexity of Indian traditions to Western academic audiences while serving as a comprehensive reference for Indian scholars seeking systematic exposition of their intellectual heritage.

The work appeared during the comparative philosophy movement associated with scholars like Paul Deussen (who translated the Upanishads and drew parallels with Schopenhauer), Hermann Oldenberg (who studied early Buddhism and Vedic religion), and George Thibaut (who translated Shankara's and Ramanuja's Brahma-sutra commentaries for the Sacred Books of the East series). These scholars sought to analyze Indian philosophical systems using Western philosophical categories while respecting their distinctive concerns and methods. Dasgupta's familiarity with both Sanskrit commentarial traditions—where philosophical arguments developed through centuries of exegesis—and European philosophical discourse positioned him to mediate between these intellectual worlds. His systematic analysis of epistemological debates (particularly pramana theory), metaphysical arguments about substance and causation, and logical structures paralleled contemporary European philosophy's analytical turn while remaining grounded in Indian texts' own conceptual frameworks and methodological assumptions. The *History* thus participated in both the professionalization of Indian philosophy as an academic discipline and the broader project of comparative philosophy that characterized early twentieth-century intellectual exchange between Europe and India.

## Significance

**Contemporary Reception:**

The *History* immediately established itself as the standard English-language reference work on Indian philosophy. Volume I received reviews in major scholarly journals including the *Hibbert Journal* (F.W. Thomas, 1921), marking its acceptance by Orientalist and philosophical establishments. Volume II (reviewed by Thomas in 1933) and Volume III (reviewed by E.H. Johnston in *Philosophy*, 1941) consolidated Dasgupta's reputation for comprehensive textual scholarship combined with philosophical acuity. European scholars including Louis Renou (*Diogenes*, 1953), Helmuth von Glasenapp (*Zeitschrift für Philosophische Forschung*, 1949), and Heinz Bechert (*Philosophische Rundschau*, 1957) engaged substantively with Dasgupta's interpretations. The work filled a significant gap left by Max Müller's limited treatment and provided unprecedented detail on traditions beyond the six orthodox systems.

**Later Assessment:**

Subsequent scholarship has recognized the *History* as foundational while noting limitations reflecting Dasgupta's Brahmanical perspective and limited engagement with Buddhist vernacular traditions, materialist schools like Charvaka/Lokayata, and folk philosophical currents. The work's emphasis on textual traditions and elite philosophical discourse underrepresented oral traditions and non-elite intellectual production. Later scholars supplemented Dasgupta's coverage through specialized studies of particular schools, regional traditions, and chronological periods. Karl Potter's *Encyclopedia of Indian Philosophies* (1970-) adopted a more systematic organizational framework and broader chronological scope. Specialized monographs on Buddhist philosophy by Edward Conze, T.R.V. Murti, and others provided deeper analysis of Mahayana schools. Studies of tantric philosophy, vernacular philosophical poetry, and non-Brahmanical traditions revealed dimensions Dasgupta's framework marginalized or excluded.

Despite these recognized limitations, the *History* remains indispensable for researchers due to its comprehensive coverage, integration of diverse source materials, and sophisticated philosophical analysis. The work continues to be reprinted by Cambridge University Press and Indian publishers including Motilal Banarsidass, demonstrating sustained scholarly and student demand. Its systematic treatment of commentarial traditions and detailed exposition of technical arguments in epistemology, metaphysics, and logic provide resources unavailable in more accessible but less comprehensive surveys. The bibliography and extensive citations of primary sources serve as essential research tools for locating and contextualizing philosophical texts.

**Value for Researchers:**

The *History* functions as both reference work and interpretive synthesis. Researchers consult it for detailed summaries of specific texts, biographical information on philosophers, chronological frameworks for intellectual developments, and analysis of technical philosophical problems. Dasgupta's engagement with unpublished manuscripts and rare commentaries preserved information about texts subsequently lost or remaining inaccessible to most scholars. His philosophical training enabled sophisticated analysis of logical structures and conceptual distinctions that purely philological or historical treatments often missed. The work's integration of orthodox and heterodox schools, northern and southern traditions, and textual sources from multiple periods provided a comprehensive map of Indian philosophical terrain that subsequent scholarship has refined but not replaced.

Contemporary research increasingly situates Dasgupta's work within the intellectual history of twentieth-century Indian philosophy, examining how his interpretive choices reflected nationalist concerns, comparative philosophy's assumptions, and the institutionalization of philosophy as an academic discipline. The *History* itself becomes an object of scholarly analysis as historians investigate how modern categories shaped the presentation of premodern philosophical traditions. Despite evolving methodological approaches and expanded source bases, Dasgupta's five-volume synthesis remains the most comprehensive single treatment of Indian philosophy's historical development available in English.
