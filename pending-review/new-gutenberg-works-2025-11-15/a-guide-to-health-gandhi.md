  Mahatma Gandhi's *A Guide to Health*, first published in Gujarati in 1913 and translated into English in 1921, presents the independence leader's distinctive philosophy of health and wellness grounded in traditional Indian practices and principles of simple living. Written during his time in South Africa, the work reflects Gandhi's conviction that personal health and national self-reliance are fundamentally interconnectedâ€”that Indians could achieve political independence only by reclaiming autonomy over their bodies through indigenous health practices rather than dependence on Western medicine. The guide covers practical topics including diet, hygiene, sanitation, treatment of common ailments, and the relationship between physical and moral health. Gandhi advocated vegetarianism, natural remedies, fasting, and celibacy as means of physical purification and spiritual development. His approach synthesized Ayurvedic principles, nature cure traditions, and his own experimental practices, emphasizing prevention over treatment and self-discipline over pharmaceutical intervention. The work demonstrates Gandhi's holistic worldview in which dietary choices, bodily practices, and ethical conduct form an integrated system of righteous living. *A Guide to Health* reveals the deeply personal dimension of Gandhi's broader political philosophy of swaraj (self-rule), extending the concept from national sovereignty to individual bodily autonomy and self-care as foundations for resistance against colonial power.
