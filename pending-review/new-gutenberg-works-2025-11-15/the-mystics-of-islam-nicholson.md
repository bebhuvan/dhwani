# The Mystics of Islam

## Overview

Published in 1914, *The Mystics of Islam* by Reynold Alleyne Nicholson stands as landmark scholarly introduction to Sufism, distinguished by rigorous philological methodology, direct engagement with Arabic and Persian primary sources, and sophisticated philosophical analysis. Nicholson, who became the most eminent Western scholar of Islamic mysticism and Persian literature in the first half of the twentieth century, produced this accessible work for educated general readers while maintaining scholarly standards that established Islamic mysticism as legitimate field for serious academic study.

The work systematically examines Sufism's origins in early Islamic asceticism, its development into comprehensive mystical philosophy and psychology, its practical disciplines and contemplative techniques, and its literary expression through sublime Persian and Arabic poetry. Nicholson analyzes fundamental Sufi concepts—divine unity (tawhid), the mystical path's stations (maqamat) and states (ahwal), annihilation of ego (fana) and subsistence in God (baqa), divine love (ishq), and the sophisticated metaphysical doctrine of wahdat al-wujud (existential monism or unity of being) articulated by Ibn Arabi and other philosophical Sufis.

Unlike earlier popular Western treatments that sometimes romanticized or exoticized Sufism, Nicholson's approach combined sympathetic appreciation with critical scholarly rigor. He situated Sufi thought within broader Islamic intellectual tradition while noting parallels with Christian, Hindu, and Buddhist mysticism—comparisons grounded in textual evidence rather than superficial similarity. His treatment of Persian mystical poetry, particularly Rumi's *Masnavi* and works by Attar, Hafiz, and Jami, demonstrated these literary masterpieces' philosophical sophistication and spiritual depth.

For Indian studies, Nicholson's work holds particular significance through its treatment of Indo-Islamic mystical traditions. He discusses how Sufi orders established themselves across South Asia, adapted to religious pluralism, and contributed to Indo-Persian cultural synthesis. His examination of Persian mystical poetry's influence extends to understanding the Mughal court culture, Urdu literary development, and syncretic spiritual movements where Islamic and Hindu traditions interacted.

*The Mystics of Islam* profoundly influenced Western understanding of Islam, challenging prevailing stereotypes by demonstrating Islamic civilization's sophisticated philosophical and spiritual achievements. The work established foundation for modern academic study of Sufism while introducing educated Western readers to mystical dimension of Islam often ignored in political and historical accounts.

## Author Background: Reynold Alleyne Nicholson (1868-1945)

### Academic Formation and Persian Scholarship

Reynold Alleyne Nicholson was born in 1868 in Keighley, Yorkshire. He studied at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he developed expertise in Arabic and Persian languages that would underpin his scholarly career. Nicholson's academic training coincided with late nineteenth-century expansion of Oriental studies in European universities, when philological analysis of Eastern texts was establishing these traditions as subjects for rigorous scholarly investigation.

His Cambridge education provided foundation in classical philology's methods—critical edition of texts, linguistic analysis, historical contextualization—which he applied to Arabic and Persian literature. This methodological rigor distinguished his work from earlier Orientalist writings that often relied on translated sources or impressionistic interpretation.

### Major Scholarly Contributions

Nicholson's career produced foundational scholarship on Islamic mysticism and Persian literature:

**Translations**: He translated major Sufi texts into English, including:
- Rumi's *Masnavi* (complete translation with commentary, published 1925-1940)
- *Selected Poems from the Divan-i Shams-i Tabriz* by Rumi
- *The Kashf al-Mahjub* by Hujwiri, earliest Persian treatise on Sufism

**Critical Editions**: Nicholson produced scholarly editions of Arabic and Persian mystical texts, establishing reliable texts for academic study.

**Studies in Islamic Mysticism** (1921): His most substantial scholarly work, examining three major Sufi figures—Abu Sa'id ibn Abi'l-Khayr, Ibn al-Farid, and Ibn al-Arabi—with extensive analysis of wahdat al-wujud doctrine.

**A Literary History of the Arabs** (1907): Comprehensive survey including substantial treatment of Sufi literature within broader Arabic literary tradition.

### Academic Position and Influence

Nicholson served as lecturer in Persian at Cambridge University, training subsequent generations of scholars. His appointment as Sir Thomas Adams's Professor of Arabic (1926-1933) recognized his scholarly eminence. His students and intellectual descendants established Islamic studies programs at universities across Europe and North America, extending his influence throughout twentieth-century Western Islamic scholarship.

Nicholson's combination of linguistic expertise, philosophical sophistication, and sympathetic but critical approach set standards for academic study of Sufism. His work demonstrated that Islamic mysticism deserved serious intellectual engagement comparable to study of Christian mysticism, Hindu Vedanta, or Buddhist philosophy.

## Historical Development of Sufism

### Origins and Etymology

Nicholson examines Sufism's origins in early Islamic ascetic movements. The term "Sufi" (صوفي) derives most likely from *suf* (wool), referring to coarse woolen garments worn by early ascetics in imitation of Christian hermits and as symbol of poverty and renunciation. Alternative etymologies—from Greek *sophos* (wisdom) or Arabic *safa* (purity)—lack linguistic evidence but reflect Sufi associations with wisdom and spiritual purity.

Early Sufis emphasized renunciation of worldly attachments, fear of divine judgment, and scrupulous observance of religious law. Figures like Hasan al-Basri (d. 728) represented this early ascetic phase, characterized by rigorism, fear (khawf), and ethical severity rather than later emphasis on divine love and mystical union.

### Transformation from Asceticism to Mysticism

Nicholson traces Sufism's transformation from ascetic renunciation to mystical love and union with God. Key developments included:

**Rabia al-Adawiyya** (d. 801): Female saint whose teaching on disinterested divine love—loving God neither from fear of hell nor hope of heaven, but for God's own sake—shifted emphasis from fear to love as spiritual path's foundation.

**Emergence of Love Mysticism**: Subsequent Sufis developed elaborate theologies and psychologies of divine love, presenting love as both means and goal of spiritual journey. God becomes simultaneously Lover (drawing souls toward divine beauty) and Beloved (object of mystical longing).

**Integration of Philosophy**: Sufis engaged with Greek philosophy transmitted through translations into Arabic—Neoplatonism, Aristotelian metaphysics, Hermetic texts—synthesizing philosophical concepts with Islamic revelation and mystical experience. This intellectual sophistication distinguished later Sufism from simple piety.

**Institutionalization**: By eleventh-twelfth centuries, Sufism organized into formal orders (tariqas) with established doctrines, disciplinary practices, chains of transmission from master to disciple (silsila), and distinctive rituals including dhikr (remembrance of God) and sama (spiritual audition/music).

### Al-Ghazali's Legitimation

Nicholson emphasizes Abu Hamid al-Ghazali's (d. 1111) crucial role legitimizing Sufism within mainstream Islamic theology and law. Al-Ghazali, prestigious theologian and jurist, experienced spiritual crisis leading him to abandon academic position and pursue mystical path. His major work *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of Religious Sciences) systematically integrated Sufi spirituality with Islamic law (Sharia) and theology (Kalam), arguing that external religious observance requires internal spiritual transformation for authentic Islamic practice.

Al-Ghazali's authority made Sufism respectable among scholars who had viewed it with suspicion as potentially heterodox innovation. His framework distinguishing between mysticism's legitimate forms (compatible with orthodox practice) and heretical extremes (violating Sharia) provided criteria for evaluating Sufi teachings.

## Fundamental Sufi Concepts and Practices

### Tawhid: Divine Unity

Nicholson examines tawhid (divine unity), Islam's fundamental doctrine, as interpreted by Sufis. While all Muslims affirm God's oneness, Sufis develop this into sophisticated metaphysical and experiential realization:

**Exoteric Tawhid**: Ordinary believers affirm God's oneness theoretically, distinguishing Creator from creation while believing in God's unity.

**Esoteric Tawhid**: Mystics realize God's unity experientially through direct perception that only God truly exists. This mystical realization transcends intellectual assent, representing consciousness transformation where perception of multiplicity dissolves into awareness of underlying divine unity.

This interpretation risks appearing to violate orthodox separation between Creator and creation, generating controversy throughout Sufi history. Nicholson analyzes how different Sufis navigated this theological tension—some emphasizing careful distinction between God and creation despite mystical union, others boldly asserting existential monism that scandalized orthodox authorities.

### The Mystical Path: Maqamat and Ahwal

Nicholson systematically presents Sufi psychology mapping spiritual development through:

**Maqamat** (Stations): Permanent spiritual attainments acquired through disciplined practice. Common stations include:
- Tawba (repentance): Turning from sin toward God
- Wara' (scrupulousness): Avoiding doubtful matters
- Zuhd (renunciation): Detachment from worldly goods
- Faqr (poverty): Spiritual poverty and need for God
- Sabr (patience): Enduring difficulties without complaint
- Tawakkul (trust): Absolute reliance on God
- Rida (satisfaction): Contentment with divine decree

Each station represents stable achievement marking progress along the path, requiring sustained effort and practice to attain and maintain.

**Ahwal** (States): Temporary spiritual conditions granted by divine grace rather than human effort. States include:
- Qurb (nearness): Sense of proximity to God
- Mahabba (love): Divine love
- Khawf (fear): Reverential fear
- Raja' (hope): Hope in divine mercy
- Shawq (longing): Yearning for God
- Uns (intimacy): Familiar companionship with God
- Sukr (intoxication): Ecstatic overwhelming by divine presence

States come and go according to divine will, representing momentary gifts. Advanced mystics may achieve permanent states, but generally states remain transient experiences rather than stable attainments.

This systematic psychology provided framework for understanding and guiding spiritual development, enabling Sufi masters to assess disciples' progress and prescribe appropriate practices.

### Fana and Baqa

Nicholson provides sophisticated analysis of central Sufi concepts:

**Fana** (Annihilation): The dissolution of individual ego-consciousness in God. This doesn't mean physical death or loss of consciousness but transcendence of separate self-identity. The mystic realizes that individual existence is illusory—only God truly exists. Personal attributes, desires, and self-will are annihilated, leaving only divine consciousness.

Nicholson distinguishes stages of fana:
- Fana of actions: One's actions become God's actions
- Fana of attributes: One's attributes dissolve into divine attributes
- Fana of essence: One's very being dissolves into divine Being

**Baqa** (Subsistence): Following fana, the mystic subsists in God. Having transcended limited ego, consciousness remains but now identified with or transparent to divine reality. The saint lives in the world, performing actions, but from state of unity with God rather than separate selfhood.

This paradoxical teaching—that self must be annihilated yet consciousness continues—represents Sufism's central mystical claim, parallel to Hindu realization of Atman-Brahman identity and Buddhist nirvana.

### Controversial Utterances: Shath

Nicholson examines shathiyat—ecstatic utterances of mystics in states of fana that appear blasphemous if taken literally:

**Al-Hallaj's "Ana al-Haqq"**: Mansur al-Hallaj's exclamation "I am the Truth/Reality" led to his execution (922 CE) for apparent claim to divinity. Sufis interpret this differently—when individual self is annihilated, only divine Reality remains, speaking through purified consciousness. "I" doesn't refer to individual ego but to divine presence manifesting through the mystic.

**Bayazid Bastami's "Glory be to Me"**: This mystic's utterance "Glory be to Me! How great is My majesty!" similarly scandalized orthodox listeners but represented, in Sufi interpretation, divine presence speaking, not individual presumption.

Nicholson analyzes debate over such utterances—whether they represent highest mystical realization or dangerous delusion, whether they should be concealed from uninitiated masses or publicly proclaimed. These controversies illuminated ongoing tension between mystical experience and religious law, between esoteric realization and exoteric propriety.

## Wahdat al-Wujud: Unity of Being

### Ibn Arabi's Metaphysical System

Nicholson provides detailed examination of wahdat al-wujud (unity of being or existential monism), most sophisticated and controversial Sufi philosophical doctrine, systematically articulated by Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi (1165-1240).

**Fundamental Thesis**: Only God (al-Haqq, the Real) truly exists. Creation doesn't constitute independently existing reality separate from God but rather represents divine self-manifestation—God's attributes and names becoming manifest in apparent multiplicity. The universe is theophany, God's self-disclosure.

**Divine Names and Attributes**: God possesses infinite attributes and names—Merciful, Just, Beautiful, Majestic, etc. Creation provides locus for these attributes' manifestation. Without creation, divine attributes would remain potential, unmanifested. Therefore, creation is necessary for God's self-knowledge and self-expression.

**Perfect Human (al-Insan al-Kamil)**: The realized mystic or perfect saint serves as comprehensive mirror reflecting all divine attributes. Such individuals achieve complete consciousness of their essential unity with divine reality while maintaining phenomenal distinction necessary for this reflection to occur.

**Levels of Being**: Ibn Arabi articulates complex cosmology with multiple levels of reality:
- Absolute Essence (dhat): God beyond all attributes and relations
- Divine Names and Attributes: God's first self-differentiation
- Subtle/Spiritual Realms: Progressively denser manifestations
- Material World: Densest, most veiled level of divine manifestation

### Controversy and Orthodox Criticism

Nicholson discusses intense controversy wahdat al-wujud generated:

**Orthodox Objections**: Critics argued this doctrine:
- Obliterates distinction between Creator and creation fundamental to Islam
- Implies pantheism incompatible with transcendent monotheism
- Undermines religious law's significance (if only God exists, what meaning do human actions and moral responsibility possess?)
- Appears to make God responsible for evil as well as good

**Sufi Defense**: Supporters argued:
- The doctrine represents experiential realization, not mere theory
- It maintains paradoxical unity-in-distinction—God both transcends and manifests in creation
- It provides most rigorous understanding of divine unity (tawhid)
- Properly understood, it intensifies rather than weakens religious practice by recognizing all acts as divine self-manifestation

This debate shaped subsequent Islamic intellectual history, with some schools embracing wahdat al-wujud and others categorically rejecting it.

### Comparison with Hindu Vedanta

Nicholson notes striking parallels between wahdat al-wujud and Hindu Vedantic philosophy, particularly Advaita (non-dualist) Vedanta articulated by Shankara:

**Vedantic Non-Dualism**: Teaching that Brahman (ultimate reality) alone truly exists; the phenomenal world is maya (illusion or provisional appearance); individual self (Atman) is identical with Brahman.

**Structural Parallels**: Both systems assert:
- Ultimate reality is singular, infinite, attributeless
- Phenomenal multiplicity is illusory or secondary
- Individual consciousness can realize identity with ultimate reality
- This realization constitutes liberation

**Historical Questions**: Nicholson considers whether these parallels resulted from:
- Universal mystical experience interpreted through different cultural-religious frameworks
- Historical influence and transmission (Sufism borrowing from Vedanta through cultural contact in India)
- Independent development of similar philosophical solutions to common metaphysical questions

While evidence for direct historical influence remains limited, the conceptual parallels suggest either universal mystical patterns or intellectual exchange between Indian and Islamic philosophical traditions, particularly relevant in Indian Sufi context.

## Persian Mystical Poetry

### Rumi and the Masnavi

Nicholson devotes substantial attention to Jalal al-Din Rumi (1207-1273), whose *Masnavi-yi Ma'navi* (Spiritual Couplets) he calls "the Quran in Persian." The *Masnavi*'s six books containing approximately 26,000 couplets present Sufi teachings through stories, parables, Quranic interpretation, and philosophical exposition.

**Literary Characteristics**: Rumi's poetry combines:
- Narrative skill in storytelling
- Philosophical sophistication in presenting abstract concepts
- Devotional intensity and spiritual yearning
- Humor, wit, and psychological insight
- Rich symbolism operating on multiple interpretive levels

**Central Themes**:
- Divine love as transformative force
- The soul's journey from separation to union
- Transcendence of rational intellect through mystical intuition
- Death of ego as prerequisite for spiritual realization
- The beloved (representing God) as simultaneously near and far
- Wine, intoxication, and madness as metaphors for spiritual states

Nicholson's later complete translation of the *Masnavi* with extensive commentary (1925-1940) represented monumental contribution to making this masterpiece accessible to Western readers.

### Attar, Hafiz, and the Persian Tradition

**Farid al-Din Attar** (c. 1145-1221): Nicholson examines Attar's allegorical *Conference of the Birds* (*Mantiq al-Tayr*), narrating thirty birds' quest for their king, the Simorgh. After arduous journey through seven valleys representing spiritual stages, the surviving birds discover they themselves are the Simorgh (Persian: *si murgh* = thirty birds). This allegory beautifully expresses realization that seeker and sought are ultimately one—divine reality resides within purified consciousness.

**Hafiz** (c. 1315-1390): The Shiraz poet's ghazals (lyric poems) achieve remarkable ambiguity, simultaneously celebrating wine, beauty, and earthly love while serving as vehicles for mystical meaning. Nicholson discusses how Hafiz's verses resist definitive interpretation—are they literal or allegorical? Does the wine represent actual wine or spiritual intoxication? Does the beloved represent human or divine beauty?

This interpretive openness represents sophisticated poetic technique reflecting Sufi teaching that divine beauty manifests through earthly forms—spiritual and physical love aren't absolutely separate but exist on continuum.

## Sufism in India and Indo-Islamic Synthesis

### Establishment of Sufi Orders in South Asia

Nicholson discusses how Sufi orders established themselves across India from twelfth century onward, becoming crucial vehicles for Islam's spread and cultural adaptation to South Asian context:

**Chishti Order**: Founded in India by Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti (1141-1236), who established himself in Ajmer, Rajasthan. Chishti Sufis emphasized:
- Renunciation and poverty
- Service to humanity regardless of religion
- Devotional music (sama, qawwali)
- Independence from political authority
- Tolerance and accommodation toward Hindu practices and beliefs

The Chishti order produced major saints including Nizamuddin Auliya of Delhi and attracted both Muslim and Hindu devotees, facilitating Islam's cultural integration.

**Other Orders**: Suhrawardi, Qadiri, and later Naqshbandi orders also established themselves, each with distinctive emphases but collectively contributing to Indian Islamic culture's mystical character.

### Syncretic Tendencies and Religious Pluralism

Nicholson examines how Indian Sufism adapted to South Asian religious pluralism:

**Shared Sacred Geography**: Sufi shrines (dargahs) attracted devotees across religious boundaries. The practice of visiting saints' tombs, seeking blessings, and celebrating annual death-anniversaries (urs) created popular religious culture transcending sectarian divisions.

**Conceptual Convergence**: Sufi concepts found resonances in Hindu traditions:
- Fana paralleled Vedantic realization of Brahman
- Divine love (ishq) resembled bhakti devotion
- Guru-disciple relationship was common to both traditions
- Ascetic renunciation and world-transcendence appeared in both

**Persian Literary Influence**: Persian became language of culture and administration in Mughal India. Persian mystical poetry by Rumi, Hafiz, Saadi, and others circulated widely, influencing both Muslim and Hindu literary production. Urdu emerged partly from interaction between Persian and local languages, inheriting Persian mystical vocabulary and poetic conventions.

**Syncretic Figures**: Poet-saints like Kabir (1440-1518) and Guru Nanak (1469-1539) drew on both Islamic and Hindu traditions, criticizing religious formalism while celebrating devotional love and direct spiritual experience. While not strictly Sufi, they exemplified syncretic spirituality flourishing where Islamic and Hindu traditions interacted.

### Nicholson's Comparative Perspective

Nicholson's treatment of Indo-Islamic Sufism recognizes both historical importance and philosophical implications:

**Cultural Synthesis**: He acknowledges Sufism's role in facilitating Hindu-Muslim cultural exchange and creating syncretic traditions characterizing pre-modern Indian civilization.

**Philosophical Parallels**: He notes conceptual similarities between Sufi and Hindu mysticism, suggesting either universal mystical patterns, historical influence and borrowing, or independent development of similar solutions to common spiritual-philosophical questions.

**Academic Significance**: His attention to Indian Sufism established it as legitimate subject for scholarly study, not merely peripheral development but central expression of Islamic mystical tradition's adaptability and philosophical sophistication.

## Scholarly Methodology and Academic Contribution

### Philological Rigor

Nicholson's approach exemplified rigorous philological methodology:

**Primary Source Engagement**: Direct reading of Arabic and Persian texts rather than reliance on translations or secondary sources, enabling accurate representation of Sufi concepts in original linguistic and cultural contexts.

**Critical Text Editing**: Establishing reliable editions of manuscripts, comparing variants, and producing authoritative texts for scholarly analysis.

**Historical Contextualization**: Situating Sufi thinkers within broader Islamic intellectual history, showing connections to theology (Kalam), philosophy (Falsafa), jurisprudence (Fiqh), and Quranic exegesis (Tafsir).

### Comparative Religion Approach

While grounded in philological expertise, Nicholson employed comparative methodology:

**Cross-Cultural Parallels**: He noted similarities between Islamic, Christian, Hindu, and Buddhist mysticism, suggesting universal mystical patterns while respecting each tradition's distinctive theological framework.

**Critical Comparison**: His comparisons rested on textual evidence and conceptual analysis rather than superficial resemblance or romantic universalism, maintaining scholarly rigor while exploring cross-cultural connections.

### Influence on Islamic Studies

*The Mystics of Islam* and Nicholson's broader scholarly corpus:

**Established Academic Field**: Demonstrated that Islamic mysticism deserved serious scholarly attention comparable to study of Christian mysticism or Hindu Vedanta, contributing to Islamic studies' development as legitimate academic discipline.

**Challenged Orientalist Stereotypes**: By revealing Islamic civilization's philosophical and spiritual sophistication, countered reductive Western stereotypes of Islam as merely legalistic, militant, or anti-intellectual.

**Trained Scholars**: Through teaching at Cambridge, Nicholson trained subsequent generations of Islamic studies scholars, extending his methodological influence throughout twentieth-century Western scholarship.

**Made Sufism Accessible**: His combination of scholarly rigor with accessible writing introduced educated Western readers to Islamic mysticism, contributing to broader cultural appreciation of Islamic intellectual and spiritual traditions.

## Reception and Legacy

### Contemporary Reception

Published on eve of World War I, *The Mystics of Islam* appeared during period of significant Western interest in comparative religion and Eastern spirituality. The work received recognition for scholarly authority while remaining accessible to educated general readers.

Academic reviewers praised Nicholson's linguistic expertise, direct engagement with primary sources, and balanced presentation avoiding both hostile dismissal and uncritical romanticization. The work quickly became standard introduction to Sufism for Western readers.

### Enduring Academic Influence

Nicholson's scholarship established foundations for modern academic study of Sufism:

**Methodological Standards**: His philological rigor, primary source engagement, and historical contextualization set methodological standards for subsequent scholarship.

**Conceptual Framework**: His systematic analysis of Sufi concepts, practices, and philosophical developments provided framework subsequent scholars refined and expanded.

**Translation Legacy**: His translations, particularly the monumental *Masnavi* edition, remain standard references, making crucial Sufi texts accessible for scholarly analysis and general reading.

### Contemporary Relevance

*The Mystics of Islam* retains value for contemporary readers:

**Historical Perspective**: Documents early twentieth-century Western scholarly engagement with Islamic mysticism, revealing both achievements and limitations of Orientalist scholarship.

**Accessible Introduction**: Provides systematic, readable overview of Sufism's major concepts, figures, and literary expressions.

**Comparative Framework**: Offers thoughtful comparisons between Islamic and other mystical traditions, contributing to interfaith understanding and religious studies.

**Indo-Islamic Culture**: Illuminates Sufism's role in South Asian religious and cultural history, essential for understanding Hindu-Muslim interaction and Indo-Persian literary traditions.
