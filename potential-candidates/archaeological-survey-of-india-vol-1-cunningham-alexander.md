# Archaeological Survey of India: Cunningham Reports (Volumes 1-3, 5)

## Overview

Major-General Sir Alexander Cunningham (1814-1893) founded the Archaeological Survey of India in 1861 and served as its first Director-General. His annual reports from 1871-1875 documented the first systematic archaeological explorations of ancient Indian sites, particularly Buddhist monuments and inscriptions.

**Key Contributions:**
- **Site Identification**: Located and documented major Buddhist sites including Sanchi, Sarnath, Bodh Gaya
- **Epigraphy**: Recorded and translated ancient inscriptions in Brahmi, Kharosthi, and other scripts
- **Architecture**: Detailed measurements and drawings of stupas, temples, and monasteries
- **Numismatics**: Catalogued and analyzed ancient Indian coins
- **Buddhist Geography**: Traced the historical Buddha's journey through archaeological evidence

## Historical Significance

**Cunningham's Legacy:**
- Established scientific archaeology in India
- Created systematic site documentation methods
- Pioneered correlation of archaeological finds with historical texts
- Trained the first generation of Indian archaeologists
- His work enabled later scholars like John Marshall to build the ASI into a world-class institution

The reports influenced colonial-era understanding of Indian history and remain primary sources for 19th-century archaeological investigations. Cunningham's identification of Buddhist sites proved crucial for understanding early Buddhism's geographical spread.
