# Shatakas of Bhartrhari (Three Centuries)

## Overview

The Śatakatraya comprises three collections of Sanskrit wisdom poetry attributed to Bhartrhari, dated to approximately the 5th century CE, though scholarly estimates range from 450 to 540 CE based on linguistic and historical analysis. Each shataka (century) traditionally contains one hundred verses, though manuscript variations have preserved over 700 verses total across all recensions, with D.D. Kosambi's critical editions identifying approximately 200 verses common to all manuscript traditions. The three collections—Nītiśataka (Ethics), Śṛṅgāraśataka (Erotic Love), and Vairāgyaśataka (Renunciation)—represent exemplary specimens of the subhashita tradition, wherein compressed aphoristic verses convey philosophical, ethical, or psychological insights through vivid imagery and linguistic precision. The work's arrangement suggests progression through Hindu life stages (ashramas): student mastering dharma, householder experiencing kama, and renunciant seeking moksha, though this teleological reading oversimplifies the collections' internal tensions and ambivalences.

Bhartrhari's verses employ predominantly Arya meter with occasional variations to Vasantatilaka, Sragdhara, and Mandakranta for thematic emphasis, demonstrating technical mastery while maintaining accessibility. Each verse functions as autonomous unit suitable for contemplative reading, memorization, and practical application, distinguishing the shataka genre from narrative or systematic philosophical texts. The work's influence on subsequent Sanskrit literature proved substantial, establishing models for gnomic poetry that combined keen observation of human nature with moral reflection, spawning numerous imitations and interpolations that complicated textual transmission. Bhartrhari's subhashitas achieved proverbial status in Sanskrit literary culture, frequently quoted in commentaries, anthologies, and vernacular adaptations across regional Indian languages.

## About the Author—Bhartrhari

Bhartrhari's biographical details remain contested among scholars, with traditional accounts, legendary narratives, and historical evidence presenting conflicting portraits. Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Yi-Jing (635-713 CE) claimed Bhartrhari was Buddhist and provided specific biographical details, though modern researchers have demonstrated errors in Yi-Jing's chronology and religious identification. Traditional Hindu sources identify Bhartrhari as elder brother of King Vikramaditya of Ujjain, though this association likely represents legendary accretion rather than historical fact. The most persistent biographical legend narrates that Bhartrhari was a king who renounced his throne seven times to become a monk, repeatedly drawn back to worldly life before achieving final renunciation—a narrative that mirrors the existential tensions pervading the three Shatakas between worldly engagement and spiritual withdrawal.

Scholarly debate continues regarding whether Bhartrhari the poet should be identified with Bhartrhari the grammarian-philosopher who composed the Vākyapadīya, a sophisticated linguistic treatise exploring relationships between language, cognition, and ultimate reality through the concept of Shabda-Brahman (word as Brahman). Some scholars argue stylistic, philosophical, and chronological differences indicate distinct individuals sharing the same name, while others cite the Shatakas' sophisticated deployment of Sanskrit grammatical resources and philosophical vocabulary as evidence for unified authorship. The Vākyapadīya's approximately 635 verses in three parts, along with the Mahābhāṣyatīkā commentary on Patanjali's grammatical work, demonstrate profound engagement with linguistic philosophy asserting that understanding grammar enables spiritual liberation through proper comprehension of reality's verbal structure.

Internal evidence from the Shatakas suggests the author was a courtier-poet rather than king, given frequent references to the degradations of royal service, strained king-counselor relationships, and the psychological toll of patronage dependence. Verses celebrate honorable poverty over servile wealth, suggesting firsthand knowledge of court dynamics from subordinate rather than sovereign position. The biographical uncertainty enhances rather than diminishes the work's philosophical significance, as the verses' existential authenticity derives from articulated tensions rather than authorial credentials.

## The Work

The Nītiśataka (Century on Ethics) addresses practical wisdom for navigating social hierarchies, power dynamics, and moral complexities through keen observations grounded in concrete situations. Scholar Budhendra's classification identified ten thematic sections (paddhati) addressing fools, wisdom, pride, wealth, wicked people, virtue, and fate, though manuscript variations prevent definitive structural analysis. Central themes include the supreme value of vidya (learning/knowledge) as possession that increases when shared, cannot be stolen, and transcends death; the psychological dangers of court patronage requiring flattery and submission; character typologies distinguishing wise from foolish, generous from miserly; and tensions between fate (prarabdha karma) and human effort (purushartha) in determining life outcomes. Vivid imagery drawn from nature, courtly observation, and everyday life grounds ethical reflection in recognizable human experience, avoiding abstract moralizing. Memento mori verses on time's passage and life's brevity create urgency for meaningful action, transitioning toward the Vairāgyaśataka's renunciant conclusions.

The Śṛṅgāraśataka (Century on Erotic Love) explores romantic passion, physical beauty, separation's suffering, and desire's overwhelming power through Sanskrit erotic poetry's conventional vocabulary (alamkara) while achieving psychological depth suggesting lived experience. Standard metaphors compare the beloved to lotus, moon, doe, and elephant; descriptions employ conventional imagery for eyes, breasts, hips, and gait; scenarios follow traditional patterns of meeting, union, quarrel, and separation. Yet Bhartrhari achieves distinctive voice through emotional authenticity and awareness of beauty's transience underlying celebratory surface. Love appears as force reducing dignified persons to desperate states, obliterating reason and self-preservation, yet worthy of celebration as testimony to life's intensity. The collection's treatment of viraha (separation in love) demonstrates mastery of major Sanskrit poetic theme through verses depicting longing's psychological torment. Distinctively, these verses maintain undertone recognizing desire's futility and impermanence even while surrendering to passion, creating complex tone linking erotic celebration to existential concerns pervading all three collections.

The Vairāgyaśataka (Century on Renunciation) articulates world-weariness following disillusionment with sensual pleasure and worldly achievement, expressing longing for liberation from cycles of desire and disappointment. Verses employ vivid imagery depicting existence's vanity: youth withering, health deteriorating, death approaching, worldly accomplishments proving empty, fame uncertain, sensual pleasures leaving regret. The renunciant life receives idealized depiction through forest meditation, possession-free wandering, contentment with minimal needs, and devotion to spiritual practice, contrasting civilization's corruption with nature's tranquility. Yet Bhartrhari's renunciation conveys personal struggle rather than serene detachment, suggesting someone wrestling with desire while advocating its abandonment—psychological complexity rare in renunciant literature. Several verses interrogate liberation-seeking's paradoxes, questioning whether spiritual ambition differs essentially from worldly craving, anticipating modern discussions of spiritual materialism. Emotional tone varies from despair to exaltation, bitter complaint to peaceful contentment, creating psychological realism acknowledging renunciation as ongoing process rather than decisive transformation.

Textual transmission presents significant complexities, with manuscripts varying considerably in verse count, arrangement, and attribution among collections. D.D. Kosambi's critical editions (1946, 1948) established rigorous textual standards, demonstrating that each shataka attracted verses stylistically similar to original core, suggesting organic textual evolution through centuries of transmission. Some scholars propose multiple authorship based on stylistic variations, metrical preferences, and apparent philosophical tensions, particularly between the Śṛṅgāraśataka's erotic celebration and Vairāgyaśataka's renunciation. Current consensus tends toward single authorship while acknowledging composition over extended period with subsequent editorial revision and interpolation by later poets working within Bhartrhari's established tradition.

## Historical Significance

The Shatakas established definitive models for Sanskrit subhashita (well-spoken saying) literature, wherein aphoristic verses combine philosophical insight with aesthetic excellence through compressed linguistic formulations and memorable imagery. Bhartrhari's achievement demonstrated Sanskrit poetry's capacity for gnomic expression balancing intellectual content with formal beauty, inspiring numerous imitative collections and anthology inclusions. Medieval Sanskrit commentaries from the 10th century onward provided grammatical explanation, philosophical contextualization, and interpretive frameworks, with commentators emphasizing Vedantic, Buddhist, or secular dimensions according to sectarian orientations. Vernacular Indian literatures extensively translated and adapted the verses, spreading Bhartrhari's themes throughout regional literary cultures in Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Tamil, Telugu, and other languages.

The work's philosophical significance derives from coherent worldview addressing perennial questions about human existence, value, and meaning through three responses—ethical engagement, erotic fulfillment, ascetic renunciation—presented as legitimate yet ultimately inadequate solutions. This structure resonates with Hindu purusharthas (life goals): dharma (righteousness), kama (pleasure), and moksha (liberation), while notably treating artha (prosperity/power) skeptically. The verses' epistemological approach emphasizes empirical observation and lived experience over scriptural authority, making arguments accessible across cultural and religious boundaries. Comparative scholarship identifies resonances with wisdom literature from diverse traditions: biblical Ecclesiastes, Stoic ethics, Buddhist impermanence teachings, and existentialist philosophy, suggesting cross-cultural emergence of fundamental human concerns.

Translation history began with 18th-century European Orientalist scholarship, with B. Hale Wortham's 1886 edition representing early attempts at English accessibility. K. Trimbak Telang's 1874 scholarly edition in the Bombay Sanskrit Series provided meticulous Sanskrit text with literal English translation emphasizing accuracy, including extensive notes and extracts from Sanskrit commentaries. Arthur William Ryder's early 20th-century verse translations prioritized readable English poetry over literal precision. Barbara Stoler Miller's 1967 edition for UNESCO's Collection of Representative Works offered facing Sanskrit text with free-verse English translation balancing scholarly accuracy and literary accessibility, published under the title "Bhartrihari: Poems" by Columbia University Press. Each translation reflects interpretive choices regarding Sanskrit's semantic compression, metrical rendering, and philosophical terminology (dharma, karma, moksha, vairagya), with translators navigating tensions between literalism and readability, technical precision and aesthetic effect.

The Shatakas' proverbial status in Sanskrit literary culture resulted in extensive quotation in philosophical commentaries, grammatical illustrations, aesthetic treatises, and devotional literature, disseminating Bhartrhari's formulations throughout Indian intellectual traditions. Influence extended beyond Hindu contexts to Buddhist and Jain literature, suggesting the verses' trans-sectarian appeal based on observational acuity and psychological insight rather than doctrinal commitments. Modern relevance derives from addressing tensions between worldly achievement and spiritual fulfillment, pleasure and meaning, integrity and corruption, authentic values and social pressures—concerns transcending historical and cultural specificity to engage permanent features of human experience.
